## Opportunity Summary

Patriot Staffing Solutions is a Midwest- and Southeast-based light industrial staffing and temp-to-perm platform with $285M in LTM revenue and $22.5M in adjusted EBITDA (7.9% margin). The company deploys approximately 2,800 temporary workers across warehousing, manufacturing, logistics, and light assembly verticals through 24 branch offices in 6 states. The founder is seeking a full exit after 22 years, and the existing management team (installed 2-4 years prior) is strong and aligned on a buy-and-build growth strategy.

## Thesis Pillars

### 1. Consolidation in Fragmented Light Industrial Staffing

The U.S. light industrial staffing market generates approximately $80B in annual revenue and is served by thousands of local and regional operators. The top 10 firms hold less than 30% market share. Patriot's $285M revenue base positions it as a meaningful mid-market platform, but significant white space remains for geographic expansion and market share gains.

The add-on strategy targets smaller competitors ($15-50M revenue) operating in adjacent geographies at 4-6x EBITDA. At Patriot's platform valuation of 8.0x, each add-on at 5x creates 300 bps of multiple arbitrage before synergies. Identified 8-10 initial targets across Texas, the Mid-Atlantic, and additional Southeast markets.

### 2. Scale Advantages in Insurance and Procurement

Workers' compensation insurance is the single largest variable cost in staffing (typically 4-6% of payroll). Larger platforms achieve meaningfully better pricing through experience rating, self-insurance programs, and carrier negotiating leverage. Patriot's current workers' comp cost is approximately 4.2% of payroll — competitive but improvable at greater scale. Each 50 bps reduction in loss ratio translates to approximately $0.7M of annual EBITDA improvement.

Similarly, health insurance, general liability, and other benefits procurement scale with platform size. The IC memo modeled $1.5-2.0M of incremental insurance savings achievable through scale and program optimization.

### 3. Bill Rate Management and Margin Expansion

The founder operated with a relationship-based pricing model and limited margin analytics. Management has identified approximately $2-3M of margin improvement opportunity through:
- Systematic bill rate reviews and annual escalation clauses
- Client-level margin analytics to identify and remediate below-target accounts
- Reduction in low-margin "accommodation" pricing for legacy clients

The IC memo modeled EBITDA margin expansion from 7.9% to 9.0-9.5% over the hold period through bill rate management and insurance savings, representing $3-5M of incremental EBITDA on the existing revenue base.

### 4. Technology as a Competitive Differentiator

Patriot's technology stack is outdated. The existing ATS (applicant tracking system) is a legacy on-premise solution, and the company lacks a modern VMS (vendor management system). Investment in technology would:
- Improve fill rates from 82% to 88%+ (each point of fill rate improvement = ~$2M of incremental revenue)
- Reduce time-to-fill from 3.5 days to 2.5 days
- Provide clients with real-time reporting and workforce analytics
- Enable data-driven pricing and margin management

## Key Risks Discussed

1. **Economic cycle sensitivity** — Temp staffing is an early-cycle indicator. In a recession, temp headcount can decline 15-25% within 2-3 quarters as clients reduce variable labor. Mitigant: Light industrial staffing (warehousing, manufacturing) has demonstrated more resilience than office staffing in recent cycles. Patriot's diversified client base across verticals provides some insulation. Downside case models a 15% volume decline.

2. **Wage inflation / bill rate spread risk** — If wage inflation for temp workers outpaces Patriot's ability to raise bill rates, margin compression results. Mitigant: Management plans to implement systematic annual bill rate escalation clauses and client-level margin analytics. Historical spread has been maintained at 30-35%. IC memo modeled 28% floor in downside.

3. **Workers' compensation costs** — Workers' comp is the second-largest cost after payroll. Loss experience, particularly in warehouse and manufacturing assignments, can create adverse cost surprises. Mitigant: Active safety program, pre-assignment training, client site audits. Current loss ratio of 4.2% is competitive. Modeled at 4.0% target with scale and program improvements.

4. **Client concentration** — Top customer represents 12% of revenue. Loss of a major client could create a meaningful revenue gap. Mitigant: Active diversification strategy targeting new clients across verticals. Client retention rate of 88%.

## IC Discussion Notes

The committee debate centered on two issues: margin risk and cyclicality.

**Margin risk:** One IC member (abstention) expressed concern that the 7.9% entry margin leaves limited room for error. Staffing businesses typically operate at 5-10% EBITDA margins, and the thesis assumes expansion to 9.0-9.5% — at the upper end of the range. If wage inflation persists and bill rate adjustments lag, margin could compress rather than expand. The deal team acknowledged the risk but argued that the margin improvement levers (bill rate management, insurance optimization, technology) are controllable and execution-dependent, not market-dependent.

**Cyclicality:** The committee discussed the macroeconomic outlook (July 2023) and whether temp staffing volumes were at risk of a cyclical decline. The deal team argued that light industrial staffing, particularly in warehousing and logistics, was benefiting from structural tailwinds (e-commerce, nearshoring) that would offset any mild cyclical softness. The committee accepted this view but noted that the downside case should be stress-tested more aggressively.

**Vote:** Approved 4-1 (one abstention on margin concerns).
