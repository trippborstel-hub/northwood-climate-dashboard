## Section 1: Governance

**1.1 Does the company have a formal climate or environmental strategy?**

Not a standalone climate strategy, no. What we have is an Environmental Health & Safety (EHS) management system that covers environmental compliance, worker safety, and hazardous materials handling across all operations. The system is modeled on ISO 14001 but we have not pursued formal certification. Given that environmental compliance is our core business — we help clients manage their environmental risks — our internal EHS practices are mature relative to companies of our size. We maintain EPA Method 537.1 compliance across all three accredited labs, hold state regulatory certifications in 12 states, and our field crews operate under documented environmental controls for every project.

Climate-specific strategy (emissions tracking, reduction targets, climate risk planning) has not been formalized. We recognize the irony — we are an environmental services company that has not measured its own environmental footprint.

**1.2 Is there board-level oversight of climate-related risks?**

No formal board-level ESG or climate committee. Environmental compliance performance is reviewed at board meetings as part of operational updates — lab accreditation status, regulatory compliance, safety metrics — but this is operational reporting, not climate risk governance. Greg Marshall (EHS Director) reports to Jason Rivera (VP, Field Operations), who reports to me. There is no direct board reporting line for sustainability or climate matters.

**1.3 Does the company have a Chief Sustainability Officer or dedicated sustainability role?**

No dedicated sustainability role. Greg Marshall serves as EHS Director, which covers environmental compliance, workplace safety, and regulatory affairs. His mandate is operational environmental compliance — ensuring our labs meet analytical standards, our field operations follow environmental protocols, and we maintain our certifications. He does not have a sustainability or carbon management mandate. We have not budgeted for a dedicated sustainability role, though we acknowledge we may need to formalize this as client expectations evolve.

**1.4 How frequently does senior management discuss climate-related risks?**

Climate as a business risk to Clearview: rarely, and only informally. Our Midwest/Mid-Atlantic footprint means we do not face the acute weather exposure that some companies deal with. When we discuss environmental topics at the senior leadership level, it is typically about the regulatory environment — EPA PFAS MCLs, state enforcement actions, emerging contaminant standards — because those directly affect our revenue and growth trajectory. We discuss climate as a market opportunity (regulation creates demand for our services) more than as an operational risk.

## Section 2: Risk Awareness

**2.1 What climate-related risks does the company face?**

Our risk profile is fundamentally different from many portfolio companies because environmental regulation is our growth driver, not our cost center. That said, we have identified the following risks:

- **Fleet fuel costs and volatility:** Our field operations require a fleet of 85 trucks (pickup trucks, equipment haulers, sample transport vans). Fleet fuel is our single largest emissions source and a meaningful operating cost. Fuel price spikes compress margins.
- **Lab energy costs:** Our three accredited labs operate 24/7 with fume hoods, analytical instruments (GC-MS, LC-MS/MS, ICP-MS), and continuous HVAC. These facilities are electricity-intensive. Rising energy costs affect our cost structure.
- **Client sustainability expectations:** We are seeing RFP questions about our own sustainability practices from larger municipal and industrial clients. Five RFPs in 2025 included some form of ESG/sustainability evaluation criteria. We can answer these questions, but we lack formal credentials (no emissions data, no EcoVadis rating, no sustainability report).
- **Credibility gap:** We are in the business of helping clients solve environmental problems. If we cannot demonstrate our own environmental stewardship, that is a credibility issue — particularly as we pursue larger government and municipal contracts where ESG scoring is increasingly part of vendor evaluation.

**2.2 Has the company experienced climate-related disruptions in the past 3 years?**

Minimal.

- **2025:** No climate-related operational disruptions. One severe thunderstorm event in June caused a 1-day shutdown at the Columbus field operations base. No material revenue or cost impact.
- **2024:** No disruptions.
- **2023:** No disruptions.

Our Midwest/Mid-Atlantic footprint is not exposed to hurricane, wildfire, or extreme heat risks in any material way. The Hartford CT and Charleston SC offices are our most coastal locations, but they are small field operations bases (combined ~35 employees), not critical infrastructure.

**2.3 Does the company have a documented business continuity or disaster recovery plan specific to climate events?**

We have a general business continuity plan that covers facility emergencies, IT system failures, and natural disasters. It is not climate-specific, but it addresses severe weather protocols for field operations (lightning safety stand-downs, flood watch procedures, winter storm equipment protocols). The plan is tested annually as part of our EHS management system. Our Midwest geography means the disaster scenarios we plan for are tornadoes and winter storms, not hurricanes or wildfires.

## Section 5: Transition Risk

**5.1 Does the company face regulatory risk related to climate or emissions?**

The opposite — we are a net beneficiary of environmental regulation. Our core business is helping clients comply with environmental standards. The regulatory landscape is strongly favorable:

- **EPA PFAS MCLs** finalized in April 2024 established Maximum Contaminant Levels of 4 ppt for PFOA and PFOS. This is the single most significant regulatory tailwind in our sector. Water utilities, industrial facilities, and municipalities must test for PFAS contamination and remediate if above MCLs. Our PFAS testing revenue has grown from near-zero at entry (2023) to approximately $35M annually (18% of revenue), and the growth trajectory remains strong.
- **State-level PFAS enforcement** in 15+ states is creating additional demand beyond federal requirements. Several states have enacted standards stricter than federal MCLs.
- **CERCLA PFAS designation** (finalized 2024) designates PFOA and PFOS as hazardous substances, triggering Superfund-type cleanup obligations. This expands our remediation addressable market.

Clearview faces no direct regulatory risk from emissions regulations. Our fleet and facility operations are well below any emissions reporting thresholds.

**5.2 Are any of the company's markets or revenue streams at risk from energy transition trends?**

No. Environmental services demand is driven by regulation, contamination events, and infrastructure investment — none of which are negatively affected by energy transition. If anything, energy transition creates incremental demand: facility decommissioning, brownfield remediation, and environmental assessment of new energy infrastructure all require our services.

**5.3 Are clients asking about the company's ESG or climate credentials?**

Yes, and this is accelerating. Five RFPs in 2025 included ESG or sustainability evaluation criteria (up from two in 2024 and zero in 2023). These were primarily from:

- Two large municipal water authorities conducting PFAS compliance testing procurement
- One Fortune 500 industrial manufacturer for environmental site assessments
- Two state agencies for remediation services

None of these RFPs made ESG credentials a pass/fail criterion, but they included scored evaluation sections on contractor sustainability practices. We answered with narrative descriptions of our EHS management system and environmental expertise, but we could not provide emissions data, sustainability certifications, or a formal ESG rating. We won two of these five contracts but scored below competitors on the ESG evaluation sections.

We believe this trend will continue. Municipal and government procurement is increasingly incorporating sustainability scoring, and our inability to provide formal credentials is a competitive disadvantage — especially ironic given that we are an environmental services company.

## Section 7: Targets & Initiatives

**7.1 Has the company set any climate-related targets (emissions, energy, waste)?**

No. We have not established an emissions baseline, so we cannot set reduction targets. Our EHS management system tracks waste management, hazardous materials handling, and environmental compliance — but not carbon emissions.

**7.2 Are there any active or planned sustainability initiatives?**

We have not launched formal sustainability initiatives, but several operational actions have had sustainability co-benefits:

- **Lab energy efficiency:** When we integrated the GreenTech lab equipment into our Cincinnati facility (2024), we replaced 3 older fume hoods with high-efficiency models and upgraded the HVAC controls. Estimated 12% reduction in HVAC energy at that lab, though this was driven by equipment modernization, not a sustainability initiative.
- **Fleet management:** We have installed GPS telematics on all 85 vehicles. The data shows fleet idle time averaging 18% of engine-on hours. We have not implemented a formal idle-reduction program, but the data is available to support one.
- **Route optimization:** Field operations managers informally optimize daily routes, but we have not deployed route planning software. Given that our field crews cover a multi-state territory, there is likely meaningful fuel savings available from systematic route optimization.
- **LED lighting:** Completed LED upgrades at Indianapolis HQ and Cincinnati lab in 2024. Remaining facilities are on a rolling upgrade schedule as part of normal maintenance.

**7.3 What would the company need to develop a more formal climate risk management capability?**

Honestly, we have the operational maturity to do this well — environmental management is what we do. The gap is that we have not turned our capabilities inward. Specifically, we would need:

- **Carbon footprint baseline:** We have the underlying data (fuel cards, utility bills). We need someone to compile it into a proper GHG inventory. This is a weeks-long project, not a months-long one.
- **Sustainability credentials:** An EcoVadis rating or similar would allow us to respond to client RFPs with formal credentials. Given our EHS maturity, we expect we would score well.
- **Sustainability narrative:** Marketing materials that tell our sustainability story — an environmental company practicing what it preaches. This would be commercially valuable for municipal and government bids.
- **Phase I ESA on own facilities:** We perform environmental site assessments for clients but have not completed one on our own properties. We should practice what we sell.
- **Fleet optimization:** Formal idle-reduction and route-planning program. The data infrastructure is in place (GPS telematics on all 85 vehicles). Implementation is a $20K project management effort, not a capital expenditure.

We do not need a Chief Sustainability Officer or a large-scale transformation. We need a structured 90-day effort to formalize what we already do well and close the credibility gap.
