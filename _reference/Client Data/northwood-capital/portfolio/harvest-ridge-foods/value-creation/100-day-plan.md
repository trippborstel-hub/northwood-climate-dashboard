**Plan Initiated:** April 1, 2025 (Day 1 = Close Date)

## Plan Overview

The 100-day plan establishes the operational foundation for Harvest Ridge's transformation from a founder-led, organically-grown contract food manufacturer to a scalable, institutionally-managed platform capable of executing an add-on acquisition strategy. The plan focuses on six critical workstreams: procurement consolidation, operational assessment, Chattanooga plant integration, production scheduling optimization, customer diversification, and food safety standardization.

**Note:** While the calendar has moved well past 100 days from close, this document tracks the original 100-day plan milestones and their actual completion status. Some workstreams that were initiated within the 100-day window remain in execution phase.

## Workstream 2: Operational Assessment (Day 30)

**Owner:** Daniel Vasquez, COO
**Target:** Comprehensive assessment of all four production plants — equipment, staffing, processes, capacity, and efficiency
**Status:** COMPLETE

| Milestone | Target | Actual | Status |
|-----------|--------|--------|--------|
| Nashville plant assessment | Day 10 | Day 8 | Complete |
| Murfreesboro plant assessment | Day 15 | Day 14 | Complete |
| Bowling Green plant assessment | Day 20 | Day 18 | Complete |
| Chattanooga plant assessment | Day 25 | Day 22 | Complete |
| Assessment report and recommendations | Day 30 | Day 32 | Complete |

**Key Findings:**
1. **Network utilization at 70%** — Significant capacity available, particularly at Murfreesboro (65%) and Chattanooga (62%). Nashville is the tightest at 76% with bottlenecks on high-volume filling lines.
2. **Changeover time is the biggest efficiency opportunity** — Average changeover between product runs is 4.5 hours across the network. Industry benchmark for comparable operations is 2.5-3.0 hours. Reducing changeover time to 3.0 hours would add approximately $2.5M in available production capacity annually.
3. **Preventive maintenance is informal** — No structured PM program. Maintenance is reactive (fix-when-broken). The Nashville Line 2 bearing failure in January 2026 was a direct consequence. Estimated cost of unplanned downtime: $0.8-1.2M/year across the network.
4. **Food waste is unmeasured** — Plants generate organic waste (off-spec product, batch rejects, changeover flush product) tracked only as waste hauling cost ($185K/year total). Vasquez estimates actual food waste volumes at 3-5% of production volume, representing $4-7M in raw material value. Quantification and reduction is a significant opportunity.
5. **Chattanooga plant operates on a different process model** — Cold-fill and high-acid production requires different equipment, scheduling, and quality parameters. Integration into the network-wide scheduling system requires custom configuration.

## Key Lessons Learned

1. **Procurement consolidation was the fastest win.** Four plants buying independently left significant savings on the table. Phase 1 delivered $1.2M in contracted savings within 95 days. This should be the first workstream in any food manufacturing platform 100-day plan.

2. **Founder-led companies under-invest in production scheduling.** The founders ran scheduling through institutional knowledge and spreadsheets. Implementing structured scheduling software — combined with changeover time reduction — is a high-ROI investment that pays back in months, not years.

3. **Multi-plant integration takes longer when process types differ.** Chattanooga's cold-fill process is fundamentally different from the hot-fill lines at the other three plants. Integration timelines should account for process heterogeneity, not just administrative consolidation.

4. **Food safety culture is a competitive asset.** Sarah Kim's rapid standardization of food safety protocols across all four plants was possible because the founder had already built a strong food safety culture. The team was receptive to higher standards because they already valued food safety. This is a hiring and retention advantage when competing for food manufacturing customers.

5. **DTC brands are a high-value, fast-growing segment.** The DTC customer acquisition cost is lower than CPG (less formal RFP process, faster decision-making) and the relationship model is stickier (R&D co-development creates mutual dependency). This segment deserves dedicated sales resources.

6. **Food waste is a hidden cost center.** The operational assessment revealed that food waste is unmeasured — tracked only as waste hauling cost. Preliminary estimates suggest 3-5% of production volume is wasted, representing $4-7M in raw material value. This is a major cost reduction and sustainability opportunity that should be formalized as a standalone initiative in the 2026 value creation plan.
