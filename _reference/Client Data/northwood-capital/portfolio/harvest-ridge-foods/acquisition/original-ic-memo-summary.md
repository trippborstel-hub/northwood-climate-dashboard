## Opportunity Summary

Harvest Ridge Foods is a Nashville-based contract food manufacturer and co-packer with $145M in LTM revenue and $21.9M in adjusted EBITDA (15.1% margin). The company operates four production facilities specializing in shelf-stable sauces, dressings, marinades, and condiments, serving regional and national CPG brands, grocery private label programs, and emerging DTC food brands. The founders (former Kraft food scientists) are seeking a partial exit after 22 years, creating an opportunity to acquire a well-positioned platform with deep formulation IP and install operational leadership to execute a buy-and-build strategy in a highly fragmented co-packing market.

## Thesis Pillars

### 1. Clean-Label / Better-for-You Reformulation Tailwind

The consumer shift toward clean-label, organic, and better-for-you food products has been one of the most durable trends in the food industry over the past decade. According to IRI data, clean-label products now represent 35%+ of center-store food sales and are growing at 2x the rate of conventional products. This trend creates sustained demand for contract manufacturers with three specific capabilities:

- **Formulation expertise** — Reformulating existing products to remove artificial ingredients while maintaining taste, texture, and shelf stability requires deep food science capability. Harvest Ridge's founding team (PhD-level food scientists) has built a formulation library of 1,500+ recipes.
- **Dual-line production** — Brands increasingly need co-packers that can run both organic-certified and conventional lines. Harvest Ridge's Bowling Green facility holds USDA Organic certification, giving the company dual capability that most regional co-packers lack.
- **R&D partnership model** — Emerging DTC food brands often lack in-house food science teams. Harvest Ridge functions as an outsourced R&D partner, co-developing formulations from concept to production. This creates high switching costs and long customer relationships (average tenure: 7+ years for top 20 accounts).

### 2. Highly Fragmented Co-Packing Market — Add-on Acquisition Strategy

The U.S. contract food manufacturing market is estimated at $75B+, with thousands of regional operators. The vast majority operate single facilities at $10-50M in revenue. There is no dominant mid-market platform in the shelf-stable sauce/condiment segment. The opportunity is to build one through disciplined add-on acquisitions at 4-6x EBITDA:

- **Identified targets:** 30+ regional co-packers across the Southeast and Mid-Atlantic with $15-50M revenue
- **Integration playbook:** Harvest Ridge's production scheduling, quality systems, and procurement infrastructure provide a repeatable integration template
- **Geographic expansion:** Priority markets include the Carolinas (growing food manufacturing hub), Atlanta corridor, and Mid-Atlantic private label concentration
- **Multiple arbitrage:** Acquiring at 4-6x and holding at 8.0x+ creates 200-400 bps of multiple expansion on each add-on dollar of EBITDA

### 3. Founder-Operators with Capital Constraints

The founders built an exceptional business — clean regulatory record, deep customer relationships, formulation IP, and organic growth from a single line to four plants. However, the company has been operated conservatively with minimal debt and no acquisition activity. The founders lacked the capital, operational bandwidth, and M&A experience to pursue an add-on strategy. Key opportunities unlocked by institutional ownership:

- Professional COO to optimize production across the four-plant network
- Procurement consolidation (four plants currently purchase independently)
- Capital for capacity expansion and automation investments
- M&A infrastructure to execute 2-3 add-on acquisitions

### 4. Margin Expansion Through Operational Optimization

The business is well-run but has clear paths to margin improvement:

- **Procurement consolidation** — Four plants currently source ingredients and packaging independently. Consolidated purchasing across $85M+ in raw material spend should yield 200-400 bps of COGS improvement.
- **Production scheduling optimization** — Capacity utilization across the plant network averages ~72%. Better scheduling, reduced changeover time, and cross-plant load balancing can drive utilization toward 80%+.
- **Automation investments** — Filling, labeling, and packaging lines at the Nashville and Murfreesboro plants are semi-manual. Targeted automation can reduce labor cost per unit by 15-20% on high-volume SKUs.
- **Waste reduction** — Plants generate organic waste currently tracked only as disposal cost. Food waste quantification and reduction represents both cost savings and sustainability value.

## Key Risks Discussed

1. **Customer concentration (top 5 = ~45%)** — The top five customers represent approximately 45% of revenue. Loss of a major customer would have material EBITDA impact. Mitigant: Average customer tenure exceeds 7 years for top 20 accounts. High switching costs due to co-developed formulations and qualified production lines. Customer diversification is a strategic priority — emerging DTC brand segment is growing rapidly and reducing concentration.

2. **Raw material cost volatility** — Key inputs (soybean oil, vinegar, tomato paste, organic ingredients, glass/PET packaging) are subject to commodity price fluctuation. Mitigant: Most customer contracts include annual pricing adjustments with pass-through mechanisms (60-90 day lag). Procurement consolidation will improve purchasing power. Exploring forward contracts on top 5 ingredient categories.

3. **Food safety incident** — A product recall or food safety event could damage customer relationships and brand trust. Mitigant: All four plants are SQF Level 3 certified. VP Quality (Sarah Kim) has 12 years of food safety experience including Tyson and is PCQI and SQF Practitioner certified. HACCP plans audited annually. No recalls in company history.

4. **Water availability at Bowling Green facility** — The Bowling Green, KY facility (organic/specialty production) has the highest water usage in the network. The facility experienced 3 water pressure drops during summer 2025 that required production schedule adjustments. Mitigant: Working with municipal water authority on supply reliability. Evaluating on-site water storage and recycling options. Not yet a critical constraint but requires monitoring.

5. **Founder/CEO transition risk** — While Meg Connelly is retained with 20% rollover, the business has been founder-led for 22 years. Customer relationships and formulation knowledge are partially concentrated with the founders. Mitigant: Connelly's rollover aligns incentives. New COO (Vasquez) provides operational independence. VP Sales (Hawkins, 10 years at company) holds direct customer relationships. R&D team retains formulation IP in documented recipe libraries.

## IC Discussion Notes

The committee focused on the customer concentration risk as the primary concern. At 45% concentration in the top 5, the loss of any single major customer could reduce EBITDA by $2-4M. The deal team presented customer tenure data (average 7+ years for top 20) and the co-developed formulation model (which creates high switching costs) as structural mitigants. The committee requested that customer diversification be a Day 1 priority in the value creation plan.

The add-on strategy was viewed as highly compelling. The co-packing market fragmentation (thousands of regional operators at 4-6x) and Harvest Ridge's established production infrastructure create an attractive platform for roll-up. The committee noted that the Southeast food manufacturing corridor (Carolinas, Atlanta, Florida) offers a deep target pool.

The entry multiple of 8.0x was discussed relative to the 7-9x range for food manufacturing platforms. The committee was comfortable given the clean-label positioning, dual organic/conventional capability, and the quality of the customer book. The R&D/formulation IP was viewed as a differentiator that supports above-average retention and pricing.

The committee questioned the Chattanooga plant's role in the network. The deal team described it as a cold-fill/high-acid specialty facility that serves specific product categories requiring different production processes. Integration of Chattanooga production scheduling into the broader network was identified as a 100-day plan priority.

**Vote:** Approved unanimously.
