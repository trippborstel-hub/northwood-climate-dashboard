| Waste Type | Handling | Volume (est.) | Disposal Method |

| General solid waste | Republic Services | Standard dumpster service | Landfill |

**Notes on food waste:** I want to be upfront about this — we don't track food waste volumes. Our plants generate organic waste from production (off-spec product, ingredient trim, batch rejects, QA samples, changeover flush product), and it's all captured as "waste hauling cost" on the P&L. I know this sounds bad, but in food manufacturing it's common to manage waste as a cost line rather than measuring volumes. The total waste hauling cost across all four plants is approximately $185K/year. I don't know what percentage of that is organic food waste versus general solid waste versus packaging waste — it's all on one invoice line from the hauler.

If you need us to break this out, we'd need to work with the plant managers and the hauler to get weight-ticket data or implement some form of tracking. Daniel [Vasquez, COO] has mentioned that waste reduction could be a meaningful cost opportunity, but we haven't scoped it.

**Wastewater Notes:** Our Nashville and Bowling Green plants have municipal pretreatment discharge permits. We monitor BOD (biochemical oxygen demand) and pH quarterly as required. We've been in compliance — no violations. Bowling Green's permit was renewed in 2024 with tighter BOD limits, which we are meeting. We spend approximately $45K/year on wastewater monitoring and compliance across the two permitted facilities. Murfreesboro and Chattanooga have lower discharge volumes and operate under standard sewer use agreements.

I want to note that wastewater is a well-managed area for us. We have good controls and haven't had compliance issues. I'd prefer we focus the sustainability discussion on areas where we actually have gaps, rather than adding overhead to something that's working.

**Compliance Status:** No FDA, EPA, or state environmental violations on record. No product recalls in company history. SQF Level 3 certified at all four production facilities. Annual third-party food safety audits (last audit scores: Nashville 96, Murfreesboro 94, Bowling Green 95, Chattanooga 93). HACCP plans current and standardized across all plants (completed Q4 2025 under Sarah Kim's leadership).

## Section 6: Customer & Supply Chain Requirements

**Have any customers requested emissions or sustainability data?**

Yes — and this is becoming more frequent. Three of our top 10 customers now require Scope 3 supplier emissions data as a condition of contract renewal:

1. **[Major National CPG Brand — top 3 customer]** (Q3 2025) — Sent a "Supplier Sustainability Assessment" as part of their annual vendor qualification process. Required carbon footprint data (Scope 1 and 2), water usage, and food waste metrics. We filled in what we could (energy consumption data) but could not provide carbon footprint figures. The procurement contact said they are "building a supplier emissions database" and that providing data would become mandatory for contract renewal starting in 2027.

2. **[National Grocery Private Label Program — top 5 customer]** (Q4 2025) — Included a sustainability questionnaire in their 2026 co-packing RFP. Required Scope 1, 2, and 3 data from suppliers. We submitted the RFP without the sustainability data and included a note that we were "working with our PE sponsor to establish carbon reporting." Won the contract renewal, but the category manager told our VP Sales that sustainability scoring would be "a significant factor" in the 2027 renewal cycle.

3. **[Emerging National DTC Brand — growing customer]** (Q1 2026) — This is one of our newer customers and one of the fastest-growing. Their founder personally asked Meg [Connelly] whether we could provide a carbon footprint for their products. They want to put "carbon neutral" or similar claims on their packaging and need supplier data to calculate product-level footprints. We couldn't provide it. They are still working with us but mentioned they were evaluating other co-packers who "have their sustainability act together."

**Our view:** I'll be honest — I find this whole area frustrating from an administrative standpoint. We're a food manufacturer, not a sustainability consultancy. Every one of these requests comes in a different format with different metrics. The amount of staff time required to chase down energy bills, estimate emissions, and fill out questionnaires is not trivial. That said, Thomas Chen [Northwood deal lead] and David Okafor have both emphasized that this is becoming a competitive issue, and I take that seriously. If 3 of our top 10 customers are asking today, it's reasonable to assume 6-7 will be asking within 2-3 years. We need to get in front of this.

**Do your suppliers provide sustainability data?** No. Our supply chain is primarily domestic — ingredient suppliers (oils, vinegars, spices, organic produce), packaging suppliers (glass, PET, labels, corrugated), and logistics providers. None have proactively provided emissions data. We have not asked them for it.

## Section 7: Physical Risk & Business Continuity

**Have you experienced any weather-related operational disruptions?**

Yes, some. The most significant issues were at the Bowling Green facility:

- **Summer 2025 — Water pressure drops (3 events):** Our Bowling Green facility experienced three separate water pressure drops during June-August 2025 that required production schedule adjustments. Bowling Green is our highest water-usage plant (organic lines require extensive CIP washing and rinsing between product runs to maintain organic certification). During the pressure drops, we had to reduce production to single-shift and delay two batch runs. Estimated production impact: ~$280K in delayed shipments (recovered within 2-3 weeks each time). The municipal water authority attributed the pressure drops to "high seasonal demand and infrastructure maintenance." We didn't think much of it at the time — we treated it as a facilities issue and adjusted the schedule. In retrospect, I understand this might be relevant to your climate risk analysis.

- **February 2025 — Ice storm (Nashville):** Nashville plant shut down for 1.5 days due to road closures. Minimal production impact (~$120K in delayed shipments, recovered within the week). This was a regional weather event, not specific to our facility.

- **No significant flooding events** at any facility. Nashville plant is on relatively high ground. Bowling Green is in an industrial park near a small creek but we've never had water intrusion.

**Flood zone status:** We have not conducted a formal flood zone assessment for any facility.

**Water dependency:** Food manufacturing is inherently water-intensive. We use municipal water for product formulation (water is an ingredient in many of our products), CIP cleaning, steam generation, and general facility needs. Estimated total water consumption: ~28M gallons/year across all four production plants, with Bowling Green being the largest consumer (~10M gallons/year) due to the organic certification cleaning requirements. Tennessee and Kentucky are not typically considered water-stressed regions, though the Bowling Green pressure drops raised some concern.

## Section 8: Energy Efficiency & Improvement Opportunities

**Have you implemented any energy efficiency measures?**

Some, but nothing systematic:

- LED lighting retrofit at Murfreesboro plant (2022) — reduced lighting energy ~35% at that facility
- VFD (variable frequency drive) installation on main blending motors at Nashville plant (2023) — installed on 3 of 8 blending motors as part of a production line upgrade. Estimated 15% energy reduction on those motors.
- Insulation upgrade on Nashville boiler and steam distribution piping (2024) — reduced heat loss, estimated $18K/year natural gas savings

**What efficiency improvements have been identified but not implemented?**

1. **Complete LED lighting retrofit at Nashville, Bowling Green, and Chattanooga** — Estimated cost: $95K. Based on Murfreesboro results, expect 30-40% lighting energy reduction at each facility. 12-18 month payback. Not yet budgeted — it keeps getting bumped for production equipment priorities.

2. **Compressed air system audit and optimization** — We have compressed air at all four plants (used for pneumatic equipment, packaging, line clearing). A vendor mentioned during a site visit that we likely have 15-25% waste from leaks and pressure inefficiencies. We haven't done a formal audit. Estimated cost for audit + repair: $50-65K. Unknown savings, but the vendor estimated $40-55K/year.

3. **Boiler economizers at Nashville and Bowling Green** — Engineering concept for recovering waste heat from boiler flue gas to preheat feedwater. Estimated cost: $120K per plant. Estimated savings: $35-45K/year per plant. Payback: 3-4 years. Discussed with our boiler service contractor but not pursued.

4. **Solar evaluation** — Meg [Connelly] has expressed interest in rooftop solar, particularly at the Bowling Green plant which has a large flat roof (~18,000 sq ft usable). No feasibility study has been conducted. TVA territory has historically had limited solar PPA options, though this may be changing.

5. **Steam trap maintenance program** — Our boiler service contractor flagged that we likely have failed steam traps at Nashville and Bowling Green (facilities are 15-20 years old). Failed steam traps waste steam and increase gas consumption. A comprehensive survey and repair program would cost approximately $25-35K and potentially save $15-25K/year.

## Section 9: Workforce & Community

**Food safety culture:** Harvest Ridge has a strong food safety culture, which is table stakes in contract food manufacturing — our customers audit us regularly, and a food safety failure would be existential. All 335 employees receive annual food safety training (12+ hours/year). Production staff receive additional GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice), allergen awareness, and HACCP training. Sarah Kim (VP Quality) has implemented a near-miss food safety reporting program.

**Community engagement:** Harvest Ridge sponsors the Nashville Food Bank's annual food drive and donates off-spec product (when food-safe but not meeting customer cosmetic specifications) to local food banks. Estimated donation value: ~$80K/year in product. The Bowling Green facility participates in the Warren County Schools STEM program, hosting tours for high school students interested in food science.

**Workforce demographics:** Average employee tenure: 5.8 years (lower than pre-acquisition level of 6.5 years due to new hires). Average age: 38. Voluntary turnover: ~14% (above food manufacturing industry average of ~12%, though improving from 16% at acquisition). Bilingual workforce — approximately 45% of production staff are Spanish-speaking, and all food safety and operational signage is bilingual.

**Connection to sustainability:** Our food donation program and food safety culture have not been framed as part of an ESG or sustainability narrative. We just think of it as doing the right thing and running a good business.

## Section 10: Additional Comments

Energy cost at $1.9M/year is meaningful but not our most pressing operating concern — raw materials (~$82M) and labor (~$38M) are far larger line items. That said, I understand the strategic importance of energy and emissions data given the customer requirements described in Section 6.

I want to emphasize the customer sustainability data requests as the most commercially urgent item. Three of our top 10 customers are now requiring Scope 3 supplier emissions data. These aren't polite suggestions — they're tying it to contract renewal qualification. Combined revenue from these three customers is approximately $34M. If we can't provide credible emissions data within 12-18 months, we are at real risk of losing preferred supplier status. I'd frame the climate/sustainability initiative primarily through this lens: it's a customer retention and competitive positioning issue, not an environmental advocacy project.

On the food waste question — I know I noted that we don't track volumes. I'm sure that looks like a gap from a sustainability perspective. From my seat, the $185K/year in waste hauling costs tells me it's not a massive cost item. But I acknowledge we might be sitting on a bigger number than I think, and Daniel [Vasquez] believes there's a real cost reduction opportunity if we actually measure what we're throwing away. I'd welcome help scoping this.

One last thing I'll flag that may be relevant: the Bowling Green water pressure drops. I mentioned them in Section 7 as a facilities issue, which is how we've been treating them internally. But in preparing this questionnaire, I looked up some data on Kentucky drought patterns and noticed that Warren County (where Bowling Green is) has been in "abnormally dry" conditions for 3 of the past 5 summers according to the U.S. Drought Monitor. I don't know if that's a trend or normal variability, but given that Bowling Green is our highest water-usage plant and handles our organic production (which requires the most water-intensive cleaning), it seems like something we should understand better.
