## Section 1: Governance

**1.1 Does the company have a formal climate or environmental strategy?**

No. NovaCare's environmental compliance efforts are focused on clinical and regulatory requirements — medical waste management, OSHA workplace safety, infection control protocols, and hazardous materials handling per state health department regulations. We use certified medical waste disposal vendors at all locations and maintain compliance with EPA and state-level clinical waste standards. Our environmental management is mature in the clinical context but does not extend to climate strategy, carbon tracking, or emissions planning.

**1.2 Is there board-level oversight of climate-related risks?**

Not specifically. Our board reviews operational risk as part of quarterly reporting — reimbursement trends, therapist retention, clinic expansion performance — but climate risk has not been a standalone discussion item. Environmental compliance (medical waste, OSHA) is reported through our operations team. Dr. Sandra Reeves (CEO) and I present to the board quarterly, but our environmental updates are limited to regulatory compliance status and any incident reports.

**1.3 Does the company have a Chief Sustainability Officer or dedicated sustainability role?**

No. We do not have a sustainability role. Our VP of Clinic Operations oversees facility management, which includes building maintenance, waste disposal vendor relationships, and compliance with health department environmental standards. There is no mandate or budget for sustainability or carbon management.

**1.4 How frequently does senior management discuss climate-related risks?**

Rarely in the climate sense. We discuss environmental compliance regularly — biohazard waste volumes, disposal vendor performance, OSHA inspection readiness, infection control audits. These are monthly agenda items for our operations team. Climate as a financial or strategic risk to NovaCare has not been a topic of senior management discussion. I would characterize our exposure as limited given the nature of our business — we operate outpatient clinics, not industrial facilities.

## Section 2: Risk Awareness

**2.1 What climate-related risks does the company face?**

Honestly, I am not sure we have assessed this formally. Our primary environmental exposure is medical waste management. We generate regulated medical waste at all 30 clinic locations and use certified disposal vendors — Stericycle at 22 locations and a regional provider at the remaining 8. This is our most material environmental compliance obligation.

Beyond that, some risks I can identify:

- **Facility energy costs:** We operate 30 clinic locations across 8 states (Pennsylvania, New Jersey, Maryland, Virginia, Delaware, North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia). Each clinic has treatment rooms with specialized equipment, HVAC requirements for patient comfort, and lighting. Energy is a real operating cost, though I have not quantified it as a percentage of revenue.
- **Weather disruptions:** Severe weather occasionally forces clinic closures. We lose revenue on closure days because we cannot see patients, and we cannot easily reschedule a full day of appointments. This has been minor historically but it is a real cost.
- **Insurance:** Our property and casualty premiums have increased modestly, consistent with market trends.

**2.2 Has the company experienced climate-related disruptions in the past 3 years?**

Minor.

- **2025:** Hurricane-related weather forced 2-day closures at our Virginia Beach and Charleston clinics in September. Estimated lost revenue approximately $45,000 across both locations. No property damage.
- **2024:** No material disruptions.
- **2023:** Winter storm in January caused 1-day closure across our Pennsylvania and New Jersey clinics (14 locations at the time). Estimated lost revenue approximately $120,000. No property damage. This was a regional event affecting most businesses in the area.

None of these events caused property damage or required insurance claims. The impact is revenue loss from cancelled patient appointments.

**2.3 Does the company have a documented business continuity or disaster recovery plan specific to climate events?**

We have a general emergency preparedness plan that covers severe weather, power outages, and facility emergencies. Each clinic has a closure protocol — patient notification, rescheduling procedures, staff communication. The plan is not climate-specific; it is designed for any event that prevents normal clinic operations. We test it annually as part of our Joint Commission readiness activities.

No. We track utility costs at the facility level for budgeting purposes, but we have not calculated greenhouse gas emissions. Our accounting system captures electricity and natural gas expenses by location, and we can pull consumption data from utility bills for most locations. We have not converted these to emissions figures.

No. All electricity is procured from local utilities across our 7-state footprint. We have not evaluated renewable energy options. Several of our leased locations would not give us the ability to change electricity sourcing even if we wanted to — the landlord controls the utility account.

**3.4 Has the company set any emissions reduction targets?**

No. We do not currently have emissions reduction targets. We would welcome guidance on appropriate targets for healthcare facilities of our type and scale. We genuinely do not know what a reasonable target would look like for an outpatient clinic network.

## Section 5: Transition Risk

**5.1 Does the company face regulatory risk related to climate or emissions?**

I am not aware of any direct regulatory risk. Our clinics operate well below any emissions reporting thresholds that I am aware of. Our regulatory environment is healthcare-focused: CMS reimbursement rules, state physical therapy practice acts, Joint Commission accreditation, OSHA workplace safety, and medical waste regulations. We are not subject to EPA emissions reporting or any state-level carbon regulations that I know of.

Our primary environmental regulatory obligations are medical waste management (RCRA and state equivalents) and workplace safety (OSHA). We maintain full compliance with both.

**5.2 Are any of the company's markets or revenue streams at risk from energy transition trends?**

I do not believe so. Physical therapy is a healthcare service driven by patient need, physician referrals, and insurance reimbursement. Energy transition trends do not directly affect demand for outpatient rehabilitation services. Our revenue risk factors are reimbursement rate changes (Medicare/Medicaid), therapist recruitment and retention, and patient volumes — none of which are connected to climate or energy transition as far as I can see.

**5.3 Are clients asking about the company's ESG or climate credentials?**

No. Our clients are patients referred by physicians and covered by insurance. They select us based on location, therapist availability, insurance acceptance, and clinical reputation. No patient, physician, or insurance payer has asked about our environmental or sustainability credentials.

We have seen one RFP from a large hospital system (for an outpatient rehab management contract) that included a section on vendor sustainability practices. We provided a narrative response about our medical waste management and regulatory compliance. It was a small component of the overall evaluation.

## Section 7: Targets & Initiatives

**7.1 Has the company set any climate-related targets (emissions, energy, waste)?**

No. We do not currently have emissions reduction targets. We would welcome guidance on appropriate targets for healthcare facilities. We are not sure what benchmarks exist for outpatient clinic networks and would want to understand what peer organizations are doing before committing to specific targets.

**7.2 Are there any active or planned sustainability initiatives?**

We have not launched formal sustainability initiatives, but some operational activities have environmental relevance:

- **LED lighting upgrades:** We completed LED conversions at 18 of 30 clinics as part of routine maintenance and lease improvement cycles. The remaining 12 are scheduled over the next 18 months. This has been driven by maintenance cost reduction, not a sustainability mandate.
- **Thermostat controls:** We installed programmable thermostats at all owned and managed locations. Clinics operate approximately 60 hours per week; HVAC setback during off-hours has reduced utility costs at those locations by an estimated 8-10%.
- **Telehealth capability:** We have been exploring telehealth for initial evaluations and follow-up visits as an operational efficiency measure. If adopted at scale, this could reduce patient travel to our clinics — our physical therapists estimate that the average patient visits 2-3 times per week for 6-8 weeks, which is a significant number of car trips. We are looking at telehealth primarily to improve scheduling flexibility and reduce no-show rates, but there may be a side benefit of reducing patient driving.
- **Medical waste reduction:** We implemented a waste segregation program in 2024 that reduced regulated medical waste volumes by approximately 15% across all clinics. This was driven by disposal cost reduction (Stericycle pricing) but has environmental co-benefits.
- **HVAC upgrades pending:** The 4 PhysioFirst clinics acquired in 2021 are still running original HVAC systems that are past their expected useful life. We have budgeted $280,000 for replacements in 2026. The new units will be more energy efficient, though that is a secondary benefit — the primary driver is equipment reliability and patient comfort.

**7.3 What would the company need to develop a more formal climate risk management capability?**

This is new territory for us. Our management team is experienced in healthcare operations, regulatory compliance, and clinical quality — but climate risk management is outside our expertise. We would need:

- **Baseline data:** We have utility bills and landlord invoices but have not compiled them into a usable emissions baseline. The data quality varies — we have direct utility accounts for about two-thirds of our clinics and landlord-provided data for the rest.
- **Guidance on relevance:** Frankly, we need help understanding which climate risks are material for an outpatient clinic network. We understand the question for industrial companies or coastal real estate, but the connection to a chain of PT clinics is less obvious to us.
- **Benchmarks:** What do peer healthcare companies report? What targets are reasonable? We do not want to commit to targets that are arbitrary.
- **Resource allocation:** Our finance and operations teams are fully utilized managing 30 clinics, 385 employees, two recent integrations, and two de novo openings in the pipeline. Adding climate reporting would require either external support or reallocation of internal resources. We would need to understand the effort and cost before committing.
