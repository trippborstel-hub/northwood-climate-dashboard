**Fund:** Northwood Capital Fund I, L.P.
**Sector:** Business Services / Commercial Facilities Management
**Deal Lead:** Catherine Zhao

## Purpose

This memo documents the exit of ProServ Facilities Management, the second realization in Fund I. ProServ was sold to a large-cap PE sponsor in Q2 2023, generating gross proceeds of $144.0M on invested equity of $57.6M (Fund I share), representing a 2.5x gross MOIC and ~22% gross IRR over an approximately 4.5-year hold period.

## Exit Process Summary

**Sell-Side Advisor:** William Blair
**Process Launch:** Q4 2022
**Process Type:** Broad auction
**Contacted:** 15 financial sponsors, 6 strategic buyers
**Final Round Bidders:** 4 financial sponsors, 1 strategic

The winning bid of $222.0M (10.0x LTM EBITDA) came from a large-cap PE sponsor seeking a facilities management platform investment. The next-highest bid was approximately $205M (~9.2x) from another financial sponsor. Strategic buyers participated but did not advance to final bids, primarily due to geographic overlap concerns.

**Key Process Observations:**
- Financial sponsors valued the scalable operating model and clear add-on pipeline more highly than strategics, which is the reverse of the Atlas exit dynamic
- The ProServ Connect technology platform was disproportionately cited in buyer feedback as a differentiator
- The buyer's value creation plan centered on accelerating geographic expansion and executing 3-5 add-on acquisitions, validating the thesis we built and the runway that remains
- Process launched in Q4 2022 in a challenging M&A environment (rising rates, widening credit spreads). Despite headwinds, the essential-service nature of the business and strong recurring revenue profile attracted robust sponsor interest.

## Value Creation Achieved

### Revenue Growth

| Component | Revenue |
|---|---|
| Entry Revenue | $95.0M |
| Organic Growth | ~$60.0M |
| Exit Revenue | ~$155.0M |

Revenue CAGR of approximately 11% over the hold period, driven entirely organically. The growth came from new office openings (7 new offices), expansion within existing client relationships, and new client wins enabled by the ProServ Connect platform. No add-on acquisitions were completed; the team evaluated several but discipline on valuation and integration capacity led to a decision to focus on organic growth.

### EBITDA Growth

| Component | EBITDA |
|---|---|
| Entry EBITDA | $15.0M |
| Exit EBITDA | $22.2M |
| Growth | 48% |
| EBITDA CAGR | ~9% |

EBITDA growth was driven by revenue scale, procurement savings (~$2.5M annual contribution by exit), and operational efficiencies. Growth was partially offset by margin dilution from newer offices that had not yet reached target utilization.

### Operational Improvements

- **Geographic footprint expanded:** 5 offices at entry to 12 at exit, covering Chicago, Milwaukee, Indianapolis, Minneapolis, St. Louis, Columbus, Detroit, Kansas City, Cincinnati, Nashville, Charlotte, and Atlanta
- **Technology platform built:** ProServ Connect — proprietary service management, dispatch optimization, and client reporting platform ($4M total investment)
- **Procurement centralized:** National vendor contracts covering top 20 supplier categories. Documented $2.5M in annual procurement savings.
- **Management professionalized:** CFO recruited, ERP implemented, regional management structure installed, standardized SOPs across all offices
- **Client retention improved:** From ~85% at entry to 92% at exit
- **Employee retention improved:** Technician turnover reduced from 38% to 24% through improved scheduling, training programs, and benefits

## Thesis vs. Outcome

| Thesis Element | Underwrite | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue growth | 8-10% CAGR | ~11% CAGR (exceeded) |
| EBITDA at exit | $21-24M | $22.2M (in range) |
| EBITDA margin | Expand to 17-18% | 14.3% blended (below, see note) |
| Add-on acquisitions | 2-3 tuck-ins | 0 (below — discipline on valuation) |
| Technology platform | Invest in dispatch/scheduling | Built full platform (exceeded scope) |
| Office count | 8-10 | 12 (exceeded) |
| Exit multiple | 8.5x-9.5x | 10.0x (exceeded) |
| Exit type | Sponsor-to-sponsor | Sponsor-to-sponsor (as expected) |
| Gross MOIC | 2.0x-2.5x base case | 2.5x (top of range) |
| Gross IRR | 18-22% base case | ~22% (top of range) |

**Margin Note:** Blended EBITDA margin of 14.3% was below the 17-18% target. This was a function of faster-than-planned geographic expansion: the 7 new offices opened during the hold period had not yet reached mature-market margins (18-20%). On a same-office basis, margins expanded approximately 250bps, in line with thesis. The margin dilution was a deliberate trade-off — the team prioritized geographic footprint expansion to create a more attractive exit story, which proved correct given the 10.0x exit multiple.

**Add-on Note:** The team screened 15+ potential add-on acquisitions but declined to execute. Targets were either priced above 8x (making accretion marginal) or presented integration challenges (different technology platforms, labor models). The decision to grow organically rather than dilute returns with overpriced add-ons was the right call.

## Lessons Learned

### What Worked

1. **Organic geographic expansion created a compelling platform story.** Growing from 5 to 12 offices entirely organically demonstrated the scalability of the operating model to exit buyers. The buyer paid 10.0x specifically because they could envision taking the platform to 30+ offices using the same playbook.

2. **Technology investment exceeded the thesis.** The original plan was to invest in basic dispatch and scheduling software. The team's decision to build a full-featured platform (ProServ Connect) was a meaningful investment ($4M) but created a differentiated exit asset. The technology generated exit interest from buyers that might otherwise have viewed facilities management as a commodity.

3. **CFO recruitment was transformative.** The founder's family member managing finance was a significant risk at entry. Recruiting Carolyn Mitchell and implementing NetSuite gave the business institutional-quality financial reporting for the first time, which was essential for the exit process.

4. **Procurement centralization delivered measurable savings.** The $2.5M in annual procurement savings was documented, auditable, and credible in buyer diligence. This is a reliable value creation lever in multi-location services businesses.

### What We Would Do Differently

1. **Moderate the pace of geographic expansion.** Opening 7 offices in 4.5 years stretched management attention and diluted margins. A more measured approach (4-5 offices) with earlier add-on acquisitions in existing markets might have delivered higher EBITDA margins at exit.

2. **Execute at least one add-on acquisition.** While discipline on valuation was correct, the team may have been overly conservative. Even one well-priced tuck-in ($20-30M EV at 6-7x) would have added EBITDA and demonstrated buy-and-build capability to exit buyers.

3. **Exit timing consideration.** The Q4 2022 process launch coincided with a challenging M&A environment. A Q1 or Q2 2022 launch (6-9 months earlier) would have accessed a more favorable financing market, potentially achieving a higher exit multiple. The decision to wait was driven by wanting to include Q3 2022 results, which showed strong seasonal performance. In hindsight, the incremental EBITDA from that quarter did not offset the multiple compression from deteriorating market conditions.

### Implications for Future Investments

- **Organic growth is a viable alternative to buy-and-build.** ProServ demonstrated that a well-executed organic expansion can generate strong returns (2.5x / ~22%) without add-on acquisition risk. This should be considered as a primary strategy, not just a fallback.
- **Technology is a value creation lever in traditional services.** Facilities management, janitorial, landscaping — these are businesses where modest technology investment can create meaningful competitive differentiation and exit premium.
- **Margin dilution from growth is acceptable if the exit narrative is stronger.** The team made a conscious choice to sacrifice near-term margin for geographic scale. The 10.0x exit multiple validated this trade-off. This framework should be applied to other platform builds where exit buyers value scale over margin.

| Metric | Atlas Compliance | ProServ Facilities |

| Entry EV | $130.0M | $120.0M |

| Gross IRR | ~35% | ~22% |

Atlas generated higher returns primarily through more aggressive EBITDA growth (driven by add-on acquisitions) and a strategic exit premium. ProServ's lower but still strong returns reflected a more measured organic growth approach and a sponsor-to-sponsor exit. Both investments demonstrated 2.0x turns of multiple expansion, suggesting that the firm's value creation playbook (professionalize, systematize, scale) reliably earns multiple expansion regardless of exit channel.
