## Section 1: Governance

**1.1 Does the company have a formal climate or environmental strategy?**

No. We do not have a standalone climate or environmental strategy. Our operations are commercial property management and building services — we manage buildings on behalf of institutional owners, but the buildings themselves are not ours. Our own corporate footprint is 7 office locations with a total of approximately 30,000 square feet of leased office space and a fleet of about 40 vehicles for building engineers and project managers. We are a services company, not a building owner or manufacturer.

That said, I want to be transparent about something I'm not sure how to handle in this questionnaire: our own operations footprint is quite small, but we manage approximately 18 million square feet of commercial property on behalf of our clients. The energy consumption, emissions, and climate risks associated with those properties are orders of magnitude larger than our corporate footprint. I'm not sure whether the properties we manage are in or out of scope for this questionnaire. For now, I'm reporting on Ridgeline's own operations — our 7 offices, our fleet, our people — and providing summary-level information on managed properties where I have it.

**1.2 Is there board-level oversight of climate-related risks?**

No formal board-level oversight of climate or sustainability matters. The board (constituted at close in July 2025) has met twice and focused on integration, financial performance, and the technology platform assessment. Climate and sustainability topics have not been a formal agenda item to date. David Morales (VP Building Engineering) reports to Marcus Webb (COO) on building systems performance, which includes energy efficiency in managed properties, but this reporting is operational, not climate-governance in nature.

I should note that we are adding a sustainability services discussion to the April 2026 board agenda, so this is beginning to receive board-level attention — though primarily as a revenue opportunity rather than a risk management framework.

**1.3 Does the company have a Chief Sustainability Officer or dedicated sustainability role?**

No. No one at Ridgeline has sustainability, climate, or ESG in their title or formal job description. David Morales (VP Building Engineering) has the closest operational connection — his team of 85 building engineers manages HVAC, lighting, and building systems across our managed portfolio, and they deal with energy efficiency as part of their daily work. But his mandate is building performance for tenant comfort and owner cost management, not carbon or sustainability.

We have not budgeted for a dedicated sustainability role. Frankly, until this questionnaire, I was not sure we needed one given our services business model. I'm beginning to think differently.

**1.4 How frequently does senior management discuss climate-related risks?**

Rarely in the context of risks to Ridgeline itself. Our own operations are office-based with a modest vehicle fleet — we don't perceive climate as a significant operational risk to our business.

What we do discuss, with increasing frequency, is climate and energy as it relates to our managed properties. Four of our institutional clients have raised energy performance and sustainability topics in the past year: requesting energy benchmarking data, asking about ENERGY STAR certification support, and inquiring about our capabilities to help them meet potential building performance standards. These conversations happen at the property management level, not at the executive level — Jennifer Osei (VP Property Management) and David Morales (VP Building Engineering) handle them. We have not escalated them to a senior leadership strategic discussion, though in hindsight we probably should.

## Section 2: Risk Awareness

**2.1 What climate-related risks does the company face?**

For Ridgeline's own corporate operations, the climate risk profile is modest:

- **Fleet fuel costs:** Our building engineers and project managers operate approximately 40 vehicles (pickups, cargo vans) across 7 markets. Fuel is a meaningful operating cost but not a margin driver — total fleet fuel spend is approximately $280,000 annually.
- **Office energy costs:** Our 7 offices total approximately 30,000 square feet of leased space. We pay utilities directly at our Denver HQ (12,000 sq ft) and as part of gross leases at the 6 regional offices. Total direct utility cost is approximately $85,000 annually for the HQ.
- **Physical risk to our offices:** Our Mountain West and Southwest locations face wildfire smoke exposure (Denver, Boise, Salt Lake City), extreme heat (Phoenix, Tucson, Las Vegas), and drought-related water cost increases. These are quality-of-life and employee wellness issues more than operational continuity risks for an office-based business.

For the managed properties (18M sq ft), the risk profile is significantly different and much larger. I want to flag these even though I consider them "client risks" rather than "Ridgeline risks":

- **Building performance standards (BPS):** Denver and Phoenix are both considering building performance standards similar to NYC Local Law 97. If enacted, large commercial buildings in these markets would need to meet energy efficiency and/or emissions intensity targets. This directly affects the properties we manage — and our ability to help (or fail to help) our clients comply could become a competitive differentiator or a liability.
- **Physical climate risk to managed properties:** Our managed portfolio is concentrated in the Western US, which faces wildfire, extreme heat, drought, and water scarcity risks. These risks could affect property values, insurance costs, and tenant demand for the buildings we manage.
- **Energy cost volatility:** Rising energy costs affect building operating expenses, which directly impact our clients' NOI and our performance as their property manager.

**2.2 Has the company experienced climate-related disruptions in the past 3 years?**

At the Ridgeline corporate level: minimal.

- **2025:** Denver experienced 8 days of wildfire smoke air quality alerts (AQI > 150) during August-September. Our HQ office operated normally, but we received complaints from building engineers working outdoors on managed properties. No formal outdoor work policy exists. Phoenix hit 118°F for 3 consecutive days in July — again, primarily a field worker safety issue for our building engineering staff. No revenue or cost impact.
- **2024:** No material disruptions. Boise office experienced 2 days of wildfire smoke in August.
- **2023:** No disruptions.

For managed properties: several clients have experienced insurance cost increases (10-15% annual) driven in part by climate-related loss trends in the Western US. Two clients in Phoenix have flagged rising cooling costs as a building operating expense concern. These are client issues that we manage operationally but that we don't track systematically from a climate risk perspective.

**2.3 Does the company have a documented business continuity or disaster recovery plan specific to climate events?**

We have a general business continuity plan that covers facility emergencies, IT system failures, and natural disasters. It is not climate-specific. The plan covers severe weather protocols for our managed properties (which are more extensive — we have documented procedures for extreme heat events, power outages, wildfire smoke, and winter storms at managed buildings). Our building engineering team is well-versed in emergency response at the property level.

For Ridgeline corporate operations, the BCP is lightweight — we are an office-based services company and most of our team can work remotely if needed. The greater BCP exposure is in our managed properties, where building system failures during extreme weather events could create client dissatisfaction and potential liability.

## Section 5: Transition Risk

**5.1 Does the company face regulatory risk related to climate or emissions?**

For Ridgeline's own corporate operations: no. Our fleet and facility emissions are well below any reporting or regulatory thresholds.

For managed properties: this is where it gets more complicated, and I want to flag something that I think is important even though I'm not sure it's technically "our" risk. Denver and Phoenix are both considering building performance standards similar to NYC Local Law 97. If enacted, these regulations would require large commercial buildings (typically >25,000 sq ft) to meet energy performance or emissions intensity targets, with financial penalties for non-compliance.

A significant portion of our managed portfolio — I estimate 12-14 million square feet across Denver and Phoenix — would be subject to these standards. As the property manager responsible for building operations, we would be on the front line of compliance. Our building engineering team would need to develop energy reduction plans, implement efficiency upgrades, and potentially manage carbon offset procurement on behalf of owners.

I'm reporting this here because it could materially affect our business in two ways:
1. **Risk:** If we cannot help clients comply, they may seek property managers with demonstrated sustainability capabilities. This is a competitive risk.
2. **Opportunity:** If we can build compliance capabilities before competitors, building performance standard compliance could become a revenue stream (consulting fees, project management fees for retrofit work).

I honestly don't know how to size this, which is why I'm flagging it for Northwood's attention.

**5.2 Are any of the company's markets or revenue streams at risk from energy transition trends?**

Not directly. Our revenue comes from property management services, not from energy production or carbon-intensive activities. However:

- If commercial office demand declines due to remote work trends (a secular, not climate-driven, risk), our PM fee revenue would be affected.
- If building retrofit requirements increase operating costs for property owners, it could compress NOI and make property ownership less attractive — reducing the total addressable market for PM services.
- Conversely, building retrofit and energy efficiency requirements could create demand for our building engineering and project management services.

**5.3 Are clients asking about the company's ESG or climate credentials?**

Yes, and this is accelerating. In 2025:

- 4 managed properties (institutional owners — 2 REITs, 1 PE fund, 1 family office) requested energy benchmarking data. We provided raw utility data but could not provide ENERGY STAR benchmarks or emissions calculations.
- 2 institutional clients asked whether we can support ENERGY STAR certification for their properties. We cannot currently.
- 3 RFPs for new property management mandates included ESG or sustainability evaluation criteria. We provided narrative responses about our building engineering capabilities but could not demonstrate formal sustainability credentials.
- 1 REIT client has explicitly stated that they intend to require their property managers to report building-level emissions data by 2027. This client represents approximately $4.5M in annual revenue (5% of total).

None of these have resulted in lost business yet, but the direction is clear. The institutional owner segment — which is our core client base — is moving toward sustainability as a standard expectation of property managers.

## Section 7: Targets & Initiatives

**7.1 Has the company set any climate-related targets (emissions, energy, waste)?**

No. We have not established an emissions baseline for our corporate operations, and we do not consider the managed property emissions to be "ours" to set targets for (though I increasingly wonder if that distinction will hold).

**7.2 Are there any active or planned sustainability initiatives?**

We have not launched formal sustainability initiatives, but several operational activities touch sustainability:

- **Building energy monitoring:** Our building engineering team monitors energy consumption across 85% of the managed portfolio as part of standard cost management. This data exists and could be converted to emissions data with appropriate methodology.
- **LED and lighting upgrades:** Our project management team has overseen LED lighting retrofits at 8 managed properties in the past 2 years ($2.4M in project fees). These were owner-directed capital improvements, not sustainability initiatives per se, but they resulted in 15-25% lighting energy reductions.
- **HVAC optimization:** David Morales' building engineering team routinely optimizes HVAC schedules and setpoints as part of building performance management. This is standard operating procedure, not a "sustainability initiative," but it has energy reduction co-benefits.
- **Electric vehicle consideration:** We have not evaluated EV transition for our fleet of 40 vehicles. Several building engineers have asked about EV availability for their company vehicles. We have not studied this.

**7.3 What would the company need to develop a more formal climate risk management capability?**

This is where I want to be honest about what I see as the real story: Ridgeline's own corporate footprint — 7 offices, 40 vehicles, 295 employees — is small. We could measure it, reduce it, and set targets relatively easily. The real climate story is the 18 million square feet of commercial property we manage on behalf of our clients.

To develop a meaningful climate capability, we would need:

- **Scope clarity:** A clear determination of what is "in scope" for Ridgeline. Is our managed portfolio a Scope 3 category? If so, we are a climate-significant company despite our small direct footprint.
- **Energy benchmarking capability:** We have the building-level energy data. We need the methodology and tools to convert it to emissions data and ENERGY STAR benchmarks. This is a $50-100K consulting engagement plus ongoing tooling.
- **Building performance standard readiness:** Denver and Phoenix BPS developments need to be tracked and assessed for impact on our managed portfolio. This is a regulatory monitoring and compliance planning exercise.
- **Sustainability services business case:** Several clients are asking for services we cannot yet provide (ENERGY STAR certification, energy audits, emissions reporting, green lease advisory). This could be a revenue opportunity if we invest in building the capabilities.
- **Corporate footprint baseline:** For our own operations (7 offices, 40 vehicles), this is a straightforward exercise. We have the data. We just need to convert it.

I think the bigger strategic question is whether Ridgeline is a "small footprint services company" or a "climate-significant platform that operates 18 million square feet of commercial buildings." The answer to that question probably changes everything about how we approach this.
