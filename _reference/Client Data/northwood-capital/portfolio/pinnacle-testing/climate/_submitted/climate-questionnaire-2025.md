## Section 1: Governance

**1.1 Does the company have a formal climate or environmental strategy?**

No. We have standard environmental compliance procedures (hazmat handling, waste disposal per EPA requirements) but no formal climate strategy document. Our operations are inspection services — we do not operate manufacturing processes or produce emissions beyond fleet and facility operations.

**1.2 Is there board-level oversight of climate-related risks?**

No. Climate risk has not been a standing agenda item at board meetings. Hurricane preparedness is discussed informally during Q3 board meetings as part of operational updates, but there is no formal climate risk oversight structure.

**1.3 Does the company have a Chief Sustainability Officer or dedicated sustainability role?**

No. Environmental compliance falls under Greg Patterson (Director of Quality & Safety) as part of his broader safety and compliance mandate. There is no dedicated sustainability role and we have not budgeted for one.

**1.4 How frequently does senior management discuss climate-related risks?**

Informally, several times per year — primarily around hurricane season planning (June-November). Paul Stevens manages storm response operationally. There is no formal cadence or structured review process.

## Section 2: Risk Awareness

**2.1 What climate-related risks does the company face?**

Our primary climate risk is hurricanes and severe weather across the Gulf Coast, where 100% of our operations are located. Specific risks include:

- Forced project shutdowns during storm events (client facilities shut down, we cannot access sites)
- Employee safety during severe weather events
- Damage to our equipment depot in Pasadena, TX (low-lying, within storm surge zone)
- Customer project delays that create revenue lumpiness
- Rising property and casualty insurance costs

**2.2 Has the company experienced climate-related disruptions in the past 3 years?**

Yes.

- **2025:** Two significant disruptions during hurricane season. Tropical Storm projected to make landfall near Beaumont caused a 9-day shutdown of Gulf Coast operations in August (actual storm impact was 3 days, but client facilities initiated precautionary shutdowns). A second storm system in October caused a 5-day shutdown affecting the Corpus Christi and Lake Charles offices. Combined revenue deferral estimated at $3.2M. Revenue was recovered in Q1 2026 but created Q3-Q4 cash flow pressure.
- **2024:** No major weather disruptions (unusually quiet hurricane season in our geography). One 2-day shutdown for flooding in Beaumont area.
- **2023:** Hurricane Idalia (August 2023) caused a 4-day shutdown of Mobile and Pensacola operations. Limited revenue impact (~$400K) as Gulf Coast TX/LA offices operated normally.

**2.3 Does the company have a documented business continuity or disaster recovery plan specific to climate events?**

Not a formal, documented plan. Paul Stevens manages storm response based on his 18 years of institutional knowledge. The informal process includes:

- Monitoring NOAA/NHC forecasts starting June 1
- Pre-positioning equipment at inland locations when a named storm threatens
- Communicating stand-down orders to field crews 48 hours before projected landfall
- Post-storm damage assessment of equipment depot and office locations
- Client coordination for return-to-work scheduling

This process works because Paul has done it for nearly two decades, but it is not documented, it is not formally tested, and it does not have a backup owner if Paul is unavailable.

## Section 5: Transition Risk

**5.1 Does the company face regulatory risk related to climate or emissions?**

Minimal direct regulatory risk. Pinnacle provides inspection services — we are not a regulated emitter. Our operations (fleet vehicles, offices) fall well below any emissions reporting thresholds. EPA regulations and state environmental rules apply to our clients (refineries, petrochemical plants, pipelines), not to our inspection services.

**5.2 Are any of the company's markets or revenue streams at risk from energy transition trends?**

This is worth monitoring. Approximately 40% of our revenue comes from refinery and petrochemical clients. If U.S. refining capacity declines long-term due to energy transition, our addressable market in that segment could shrink. However, we believe there is a counterargument: energy transition creates inspection demand during facility decommissioning, retrofitting, and compliance upgrades. Our infrastructure inspection segment (15% of revenue) is growing rapidly and is directly tied to federal infrastructure spending.

**5.3 Are clients asking about the company's ESG or climate credentials?**

Increasingly, yes. Three of our major refinery clients (combined ~$18M annual revenue) have sent us ESG questionnaires in the past 12 months as part of their contractor qualification reviews. We answered them to the best of our ability but do not have formal ESG credentials, ratings, or certifications to reference. None of these clients have made ESG credentials a hard requirement for contractor qualification — yet. But the trend is clear.

## Section 6: Client Requirements

**6.1 Have any clients required specific environmental certifications or ESG ratings?**

Not as a hard requirement. Three major refinery clients have requested ESG questionnaires (see 5.3 above). One pipeline client asked if we have ISO 14001 certification — we do not. No contracts have been lost due to ESG credentials, but we are aware we may not be invited to certain RFPs in the future if we cannot demonstrate basic ESG compliance.

**6.2 What percentage of revenue comes from clients with their own net-zero or emissions reduction targets?**

We have not mapped this specifically. Based on our knowledge of our client base, most major refinery and petrochemical clients (representing ~40% of revenue) have public sustainability commitments. The infrastructure and industrial clients (remaining ~60%) have mixed or no public commitments.

## Section 7: Targets & Initiatives

**7.1 Has the company set any climate-related targets (emissions, energy, waste)?**

No. We have not set any climate-related targets.

**7.2 Are there any active or planned sustainability initiatives?**

No formal initiatives. Informally:

- We have replaced 4 of our oldest diesel inspection trucks with newer, more fuel-efficient models as part of normal fleet turnover (not a climate initiative — a maintenance and reliability decision)
- LED lighting upgrades were completed at the Houston HQ and Pasadena depot in 2024 (estimated 15% reduction in lighting energy cost at those two locations)
- Fleet telematics installed on all 32 vehicles provides route optimization and idle-time monitoring data, though we have not formally used it for fuel reduction purposes

**7.3 What would the company need to develop a more formal climate risk management capability?**

Frankly, we have limited bandwidth and no in-house expertise for this. We would need:

- External guidance on what frameworks and metrics are relevant for a company of our size and sector
- Budget allocation for any certifications or ratings processes
- A clear understanding of what our clients and investors actually require vs. what would be "nice to have"
- Support from Northwood on prioritization — we have many competing demands during this integration phase
