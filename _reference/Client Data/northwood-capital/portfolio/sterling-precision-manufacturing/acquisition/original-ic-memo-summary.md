## Company Overview

Sterling Precision Manufacturing is a precision CNC machining business headquartered in the upper Midwest, serving aerospace, defense, and industrial OEMs. Founded in 1990, the company operates two production facilities with approximately 285 employees and 80+ CNC machining centers. Revenue at underwrite was $110.0M with adjusted EBITDA of $18.8M (17.1% margin).

The company manufactures complex, tight-tolerance machined components — primarily aluminum, titanium, and specialty alloy parts — for critical applications in aircraft engines, missile systems, satellite structures, and industrial turbines. Sterling holds AS9100 (aerospace quality management) and ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations) certifications, both of which serve as meaningful barriers to entry.

## Investment Thesis

### 1. Secular Reshoring Tailwind

U.S. manufacturing reshoring is accelerating, driven by supply chain disruptions (COVID-19), geopolitical risk (China decoupling), and federal policy incentives. Precision machining is a direct beneficiary — OEMs are actively re-qualifying domestic suppliers for components previously sourced from Asia. Sterling's 30-year track record, quality certifications, and domestic manufacturing footprint position it to capture incremental volumes without material capital investment.

**Evidence cited:**
- Reshoring Initiative data showing 2019-2020 as inflection point for announced reshoring projects
- Three existing Sterling customers had begun conversations about relocating machined component programs from Asian suppliers
- Congressional defense procurement language increasingly mandating domestic sourcing for Tier 2/3 components

### 2. Defense Demand Growth

U.S. defense budgets have grown at a 3-5% CAGR over the prior decade with bipartisan support. Key defense programs relevant to Sterling (F-35, hypersonic missiles, next-gen satellite systems) have multi-year production ramps scheduled through 2030+. Sterling's ITAR certification and existing relationships with three of the five major defense primes position the company to win larger, longer-cycle programs under professional management.

**At underwrite:** Defense revenue was 25% of total ($27.5M). The IC memo projected defense growing to 35-40% of revenue by Year 5, adding $20-30M in incremental revenue.

### 3. Margin Expansion via Operational Efficiency and Automation

The founder operated the business conservatively, with limited capital investment in automation. Approximately 40% of CNC machining centers were older than 15 years. The investment case identified $3-5M of annual capex over 3 years to upgrade and automate production, targeting:

- 200-400 bps of margin expansion through reduced per-unit labor content
- 15-20% throughput improvement on existing floor space
- Reduced scrap rates and improved first-pass yield

### 4. Add-On Acquisition Opportunity

The precision machining market is highly fragmented — thousands of small shops with $5-20M in revenue. Sterling's scale (top 5% by revenue in the precision CNC segment) positions it as a natural platform for roll-up. Identified adjacencies included precision tooling, surface treatment / coating, and sub-assembly services. Target acquisition multiples in the 5.0x-7.0x range vs. 8.0x platform entry.

## Competitive Landscape

Sterling competes in the mid-tier of the precision machining market — above the thousands of small job shops but below the large publicly traded aerospace suppliers (e.g., Precision Castparts, TransDigm, Heico). This positioning is attractive because:

- Large primes increasingly prefer consolidated supplier relationships (fewer, larger, more capable suppliers)
- Small shops lack certifications, quality systems, and financial stability for defense programs
- Mid-tier operators with professional management, quality certifications, and multi-process capabilities command premium multiples

**Key competitors cited:** Arrow Electronics Manufacturing, Meggitt Machining Solutions, Kaman Aerospace machining division (public comps for valuation).

## IC Discussion Notes

- **COVID timing:** Committee discussed the risk of acquiring during peak uncertainty. Consensus view was that the entry multiple (8.0x) provided adequate margin of safety and that aerospace/defense fundamentals were intact. Harrison noted that two competing bidders had dropped out, creating a favorable negotiating position.

- **Founder transition:** Committee flagged the founder's 30-year tenure and deep customer relationships as a key risk. Kline's recruitment and Williams' continuity as COO were viewed as sufficient mitigation. A 6-month transition advisory agreement with the founder was negotiated.

- **Automation capex:** Kapoor questioned whether the $3-5M annual automation spend was sufficient. Harrison and Okafor (Operating Partner) confirmed the capex plan was based on a detailed shop floor assessment and would be phased to minimize production disruption.

## Post-Close Assessment (as of Jan 2026)

The investment has outperformed the base case underwrite on most dimensions:

| Metric | Year 5 Plan | Actual (LTM Jan 2026) | vs. Plan |
|--------|-------------|----------------------|----------|
| Revenue | $165.0M | $168.0M | +$3.0M |

| Defense Revenue % | 35-40% | ~38% | In range |
| Leverage | <1.5x | 1.1x | Better |

Revenue is slightly ahead of plan. EBITDA is modestly below the Year 5 target due to higher-than-anticipated labor costs (wage inflation for skilled machinists has exceeded original assumptions by approximately 200 bps annually). However, the ongoing Phase II automation investment is expected to structurally address the labor cost pressure over the next 12-18 months. Overall thesis execution is strong and the company is on track for a successful outcome.
