## Transaction Context

Sterling Precision Manufacturing was a founder-owned precision machining business headquartered in the upper Midwest, specializing in complex CNC-machined components for the aerospace and defense end markets. The founder, who had built the business over 30 years, was seeking a full exit for retirement purposes. The company was well-managed operationally but had reached a growth ceiling under the founder's conservative management approach — limited investment in automation, no acquisitive strategy, and underweight in defense (25% of revenue at entry vs. available opportunity).

Northwood acquired Sterling at 8.0x EBITDA, an attractive entry point reflecting several factors: (1) COVID-era uncertainty depressed valuations in the industrial sector during Q2 2020, (2) founder-exit transitions typically trade at a discount due to perceived key-man risk, and (3) the business had modest scale relative to its larger publicly traded competitors. The 8.0x entry multiple was approximately 1.0x-1.5x below comparable industrial platform transactions during the 2019-2020 period.

## Original Investment Thesis

1. **Reshoring tailwind:** Accelerating domestic manufacturing reshoring trend creates secular demand growth for precision-machined components from U.S.-based suppliers, particularly in defense and commercial aerospace.

2. **Defense demand:** Increasing U.S. defense spending and focus on supply chain security drive demand for ITAR-compliant domestic suppliers. Sterling was positioned to capture larger program wins with professional management and expanded capacity.

3. **Margin expansion through automation:** Significant opportunity to invest in CNC automation and lean manufacturing to reduce per-unit labor content, improve throughput, and expand margins by 200-400 bps over the hold period.

4. **Add-on acquisitions:** Fragmented precision machining market offers opportunities to acquire complementary capabilities (e.g., precision tooling, surface treatment, assembly) at 5.0x-7.0x multiples.
