## Section 3: Risk Awareness

**3.1 What climate-related risks does the company face?**

I want to answer this question as thoughtfully as I can, because I think the risks we face are somewhat different from what a manufacturing or industrial company might report.

- **Service continuity risk from extreme weather:** Our patient population — children with autism spectrum disorder — is particularly vulnerable to disruptions in routine. Therapeutic progress depends on consistent, predictable sessions. ABA therapy is not like a dental appointment that can be rescheduled without consequence. When clinics close due to weather events, we cannot simply reschedule — the disruption itself can cause behavioral regression that requires additional therapeutic intervention to address. A child who has spent months building a new skill through daily reinforcement can lose meaningful progress from even a few days of service interruption. In 2025, we experienced 3 weather-related clinic closures (2 hurricane-related in our Southeast locations in Richmond and Roanoke, 1 winter storm in the Mid-Atlantic that affected our Maryland and Delaware clinics). In each case, we observed measurable regression in treatment goals for affected patients within 2-3 weeks of the service gap. This is not an abstract risk — it directly affects patient outcomes and, over time, our clinical reputation and payor relationships.

- **Energy cost exposure:** Our 20 clinic locations all require consistent, comfortable indoor environments. Therapy rooms need to be climate-controlled, well-lit, and quiet. We are a tenant in most of our locations, so our ability to control HVAC systems and energy costs varies. Rising energy costs are passed through to us in some leases and absorbed by landlords in others — we do not have full visibility into our energy cost exposure across the portfolio.

- **Therapist commuting and in-home service delivery:** Approximately 35% of our therapy sessions are delivered in patients' homes. Our therapists drive personal vehicles to patient residences. Extreme weather events affect their ability to reach patients safely, and we have a policy of canceling in-home sessions when weather conditions are unsafe. I am unsure whether therapist vehicle use falls within the scope of this questionnaire, but I wanted to flag it for your guidance.

- **Facility damage risk:** Our clinic locations are leased commercial spaces in suburban and urban areas. We are not aware of any locations in FEMA-designated flood zones, though we have not conducted a formal assessment. I am aware that our Virginia locations (Richmond, Alexandria, Fairfax, Roanoke) and Maryland locations (Towson, Columbia, Annapolis, Hagerstown) have some exposure to hurricane remnants and severe storms.

**3.2 Has the company experienced climate-related disruptions in the past 3 years?**

Yes. In 2025, we experienced the following weather-related service disruptions:

| Event | Date | Clinics Affected | Duration | Impact |
|-------|------|------------------|----------|--------|
| Hurricane remnants (Southeast) | September 2025 | Richmond VA, Roanoke VA | 2 days | ~45 patient sessions canceled per day across both locations. Observed behavioral regression in 15-20% of affected patients within 2-3 weeks. |
| Winter storm (Mid-Atlantic) | January 2025 | Towson MD, Columbia MD, Annapolis MD, Wilmington DE, Newark DE | 1 day | ~120 patient sessions canceled. Shorter disruption — impact was less severe but still measurable for some patients. |

Prior to 2025, the company operated fewer locations and was smaller. Dr. Adams reports that there were occasional weather-related closures in 2023 and 2024 but these were not systematically tracked.

I want to emphasize that the impact of these closures is not just operational (lost revenue from canceled sessions). The clinical impact — regression in patient treatment goals — is the more significant concern. Insurance payors evaluate our clinical outcomes, and sustained disruptions could affect our ability to demonstrate progress, which could in turn affect reimbursement rates.

**3.3 Does the company have a documented business continuity or disaster recovery plan specific to climate events?**

We have a general clinic operations manual that includes severe weather procedures — essentially, a decision tree for clinic closures based on weather advisories, road conditions, and power outages. Each clinic director has authority to close their location based on local conditions, and we have a notification system (text and email) to alert patients and families of closures.

This is not a formal business continuity plan. It does not address make-up session protocols, patient communication timelines, or clinical impact mitigation. After the September 2025 hurricane-related closures, Dr. Adams and I discussed developing a more robust protocol, but we have not yet done so. I would welcome Northwood's input on what an appropriate plan looks like for a multi-site healthcare services company.

Not to our knowledge. We procure electricity through local utilities in Pennsylvania, New Jersey, Delaware, Maryland, and Virginia. We have not evaluated renewable energy options and do not know whether any of our utility providers include renewable generation in their standard mix. As tenants, we generally do not have the ability to choose our energy provider or install on-site generation.

The 2 clinics we opened in 2025 (Trenton, NJ and Hagerstown, MD) were built out with Energy Star-rated appliances and LED lighting throughout. This was a decision Dr. Adams made during the buildout process. We have not yet conducted formal energy audits at our existing locations to understand where similar improvements could be made.

**4.4 Has the company set any emissions reduction targets?**

No. We have not established an emissions baseline and therefore cannot set meaningful reduction targets. This is the first time we have compiled energy data across our clinic network. I hope this questionnaire and the associated data collection will serve as a starting point for understanding our footprint.

## Section 7: Targets & Initiatives

**7.1 Has the company set any climate-related targets (emissions, energy, waste)?**

No. We have not set any climate-related targets. This questionnaire represents our first engagement with the topic of emissions and energy tracking.

**7.2 Are there any active or planned sustainability initiatives?**

We have not launched formal sustainability initiatives. However, I can report the following:

- **New clinic buildouts (2025):** The 2 clinics opened in 2025 (Trenton, NJ and Hagerstown, MD) were built out with Energy Star-rated appliances and LED lighting. Dr. Adams made this a requirement during the buildout specifications. The incremental cost was modest — approximately $3,000-$5,000 per location — and the landlords were supportive.

- **General waste practices:** Our clinics generate primarily standard office waste (paper, packaging, food waste from staff areas). We do not generate hazardous waste. Several of our clinics have recycling programs, but this is typically driven by the building's waste management setup, not by a Keystone-level policy.

- **HVAC awareness:** We believe most of our clinics use electric heat pump systems, but we have not conducted facility-level HVAC assessments. Our lease agreements vary — in some locations we manage the HVAC system, in others the landlord does. I do not have a complete picture of the HVAC equipment across our portfolio, and this is something I would like to address as part of a broader facilities management initiative.

**7.3 What would the company need to develop a more formal climate risk management capability?**

Honestly, quite a lot. We are still building basic operational infrastructure, and sustainability is not yet on our roadmap. That said, I believe the following would be valuable:

- **Baseline energy data:** This questionnaire process has been a useful first step. We now have at least a rough picture of our energy consumption across the network. Improving the quality of this data — particularly at landlord-managed sites — should be a priority.

- **New clinic sustainability criteria:** We anticipate opening 2-3 new clinics in 2026 and would welcome guidance on incorporating sustainability criteria into site selection and buildout specifications. The Energy Star and LED approach used in our 2025 buildouts could be formalized as a standard, and we could add criteria around HVAC efficiency, building envelope performance, and potentially proximity to public transit (which would benefit both therapist commuting and family access).

- **Service continuity planning:** As I discussed in Section 3, the clinical impact of weather-related disruptions is our most significant climate-related concern. Developing a formal service continuity plan that addresses make-up protocols, telehealth backup options for ABA sessions (which is clinically complex but potentially feasible for some session types), and proactive communication with families would be more immediately valuable to us than an emissions reduction program.

- **Facilities management standardization:** As we grow, we need a more systematic approach to facilities management that includes energy and environmental considerations. Right now, each clinic is managed somewhat independently, and we do not have consistent data on building systems, energy performance, or lease terms related to utilities and maintenance responsibilities.

We do not currently have the internal expertise or bandwidth to develop these capabilities on our own. If Northwood can provide guidance or connect us with resources, we are receptive.
