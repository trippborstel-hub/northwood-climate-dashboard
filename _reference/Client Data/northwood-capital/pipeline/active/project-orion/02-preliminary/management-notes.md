## Session 1: James Whitfield — CEO & Founder

**Duration:** 2.5 hours (individual session)

### Background & Story

Whitfield founded CloudFirst in 2008 after serving as IT Director for a regional financial services firm in Atlanta. He recognized that mid-market companies were underserved by IT providers — too large for local break/fix shops, too small for enterprise MSPs. Started with two employees in a small office in Buckhead. Built the business organically to $58M in revenue over 17 years with no outside capital.

Whitfield is 55. Not looking to retire but wants partial liquidity and a partner who can bring capital, acquisition expertise, and institutional infrastructure to accelerate the platform. He has been sole owner and has self-funded all growth — there is no institutional board, no outside investors, and limited formal governance. He recognizes this as a gap.

### Impressions

**Strengths:**
- Deep domain expertise. Understands the MSP and cybersecurity landscape at a granular level — technology platforms, vendor economics, pricing models, competitive dynamics. This is not a delegator CEO — he knows the business intimately.
- Exceptional client relationships. Whitfield personally manages the top 10 client relationships. Clients describe him as a "trusted advisor" — he attends their board meetings, participates in their technology planning, and his personal involvement is cited as the primary reason for loyalty.
- Built a real business. $58M in revenue with no outside capital is a meaningful accomplishment. He has made prudent investments (SOC buildout, cybersecurity practice launch, regional expansion) at the right moments.
- Articulate communicator. Clear, organized thinker. Presented a thoughtful 3-year roadmap without prompting. Knows where the business needs to go and what capabilities it needs.

**Concerns:**
- **Founder concentration risk is real.** The top 3 clients (28% of revenue) have direct, personal relationships with Whitfield. When asked who would own these relationships if he stepped back, his answer was vague — "Rebecca and Sarah would step in, they know the clients." This is not a succession plan. It is an aspiration.
- **Limited delegation on strategic decisions.** Whitfield makes all pricing decisions, all contract approvals above $50K/year, and all hiring decisions for senior roles. The business has scaled beyond what a single decision-maker can manage effectively. There is no operating committee or formal management governance.
- **Ambiguous post-transaction role.** Whitfield says he wants to "stay involved" and "focus on clients and strategy" but has not articulated a specific role or reporting structure. This needs to be clarified before LOI. The transition plan must be structured with milestones, a defined timeline (12-24 months in CEO role, then transition to advisory), and relationship transfer protocols for key accounts.

### Key Quotes

- On competition: "The big MSPs ignore mid-market clients once they onboard them. We treat every client like our largest client. That is our moat."
- On cybersecurity: "Every client conversation now starts with security. Two years ago it was an upsell. Now it is the entry point."
- On the transaction: "I built this business from nothing. I want a partner who will invest in it, not strip it. The people here matter to me."

## Session 2: Rebecca Santos — CFO

**Duration:** 2 hours (individual session)

### Background

Santos joined CloudFirst in 2019 as Controller and was promoted to CFO in 2021. Previously spent 8 years at a $200M technology services company, most recently as Senior Controller. CPA (Georgia). She has been the primary driver of financial professionalization at CloudFirst — implemented NetSuite ERP, built the budgeting and forecasting process, and established monthly financial reporting cadence.

### Impressions

**Strengths:**
- Competent financial operator. She has built the financial infrastructure from scratch — the Company had QuickBooks and Excel spreadsheets when she arrived. Financial reporting is now institutional-grade for a company this size.
- Clear on the numbers. She walked through the EBITDA bridge with precision, anticipated every adjustment question, and provided supporting documentation proactively. The $1.3M in adjustments (founder comp normalization, one-time lease costs) are well-supported and defensible.
- Understands the business economics. She can articulate gross margin drivers at the service-line level, explain the relationship between bill rate and utilization, and describe the cash conversion dynamics. This is not just a bookkeeper — she understands the operating model.
- Organized and transparent. Data room was well-prepared. She provided 5-year historical financials, customer-level revenue data, contract renewal schedule, and detailed headcount data without delay.

**Concerns:**
- Has never been CFO in a PE-backed environment. She will need coaching on board reporting, covenant compliance, and the cadence and depth of financial reporting that a PE sponsor expects. This is coachable — she has the foundation.
- Capital structure and treasury experience is limited. All growth has been self-funded — she has no experience with leveraged capital structures, credit agreements, or lender relationships. Will need support from the Northwood team during the initial period.

### Financial Items Discussed

- Confirmed 85% recurring revenue is based on contracted MRR, not estimated.
- NRR of 112% calculated on a trailing 12-month cohort basis. Methodology is sound.
- Gross margin has expanded from 42% to 44% over 3 years — driven by cybersecurity mix shift (higher margin) and improved technician utilization in managed services.
- Working capital is minimal: ~$2.3M NWC (4% of revenue). DSO is 42 days and stable.
- Capex runs $2.0-2.5M annually. Maintenance capex is ~$1.0M (SOC infrastructure, network equipment). Growth capex has been ~$1.3M (new office buildouts, tooling).

## Session 3: Michael Okafor — CTO

**Duration:** 1.5 hours (individual session + SOC tour)

### Background

Okafor joined CloudFirst in 2019 to build the cybersecurity practice from the ground up. Previously served as CISO at a Fortune 500 financial institution for 6 years. Holds CISSP, CISM, and CEH certifications. Before the CISO role, he spent 10 years in cybersecurity consulting at Deloitte. Deep technical expertise and strong industry reputation.

### Impressions

**Strengths:**
- **Exceptional technical leader.** Okafor designed and built the entire cybersecurity practice — SOC operations, technology stack selection, service packaging, pricing model, and the compliance framework. He has the rare combination of deep technical expertise and business acumen.
- **Recruiting magnet.** The cybersecurity team (approximately 35 people) is loyal to Okafor. He has built a strong technical culture — continuous training, certification sponsorship, and a rotation program that gives analysts exposure to incident response, threat hunting, and compliance. In a market where cybersecurity talent is scarce, this is a significant competitive advantage.
- **Clear vision for the practice.** Okafor presented a detailed technology roadmap including AI-augmented threat detection, automated incident response playbooks, and managed SIEM-as-a-service. He understands where the market is going and has a credible plan to keep CloudFirst at the forefront.

**Concerns:**
- **Key person risk.** Okafor is the cybersecurity practice. If he departed, the Company would lose its technical credibility, its SOC operational knowledge, and its ability to recruit cybersecurity talent. Retention is critical — equity participation, competitive compensation, and a defined growth path are non-negotiable.
- **Limited management infrastructure below Okafor.** The cybersecurity practice has two team leads but no VP-level or Director-level management. Okafor manages 35 people directly and handles sales engineering, client escalations, and technology strategy. This needs to be addressed — promote or recruit a Director of Security Operations to create management depth.

### SOC Tour Notes

- 24/7 SOC operation in Atlanta with 12 analysts in 3 shifts.
- Technology stack: CrowdStrike (EDR), Palo Alto (SIEM), Splunk (log management), Proofpoint (email security), custom threat intelligence feeds.
- Monitoring approximately 15,000 endpoints across the client base.
- Average incident response time: 18 minutes (detection to analyst engagement). Strong metric.
- SOC capacity: Current infrastructure supports approximately 25,000 endpoints. Expansion to 40,000 would require an additional $1.5M investment in infrastructure and 6-8 additional analysts.

## Key Takeaways & Action Items

### Overall Assessment

Strong management team with identifiable gaps that are addressable. Whitfield is a capable founder-CEO who has built an excellent business but has not built the institutional infrastructure around himself. The CFO is competent and coachable. The CTO is exceptional and is a critical retention priority.

### Founder Transition — Critical Path

The single most important issue to resolve before IC. Whitfield must agree to:

1. A structured transition timeline (12-24 months as CEO, then transition to Chairman/Advisory)
2. A defined relationship transfer protocol for the top 10 clients
3. Hiring a President/COO within 6 months of close to assume operational responsibilities
4. A retention package for Whitfield tied to transition milestones, not just time-based vesting

### Key Hires Post-Close

1. President/COO — to assume operational responsibilities as Whitfield transitions
2. Director of Security Operations — to create management depth below Okafor
3. VP of Corporate Development — to execute the add-on acquisition strategy

### Retention Priorities

1. Michael Okafor (CTO) — must receive meaningful equity and a long-term growth path
2. Rebecca Santos (CFO) — equity participation and PE mentorship
3. Sarah Kim (VP Sales) — incentive alignment with growth plan
