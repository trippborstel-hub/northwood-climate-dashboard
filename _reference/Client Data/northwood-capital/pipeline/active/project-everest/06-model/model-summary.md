## Key Model Risks

### 1. Working Capital Intensity

NWC at ~22% of revenue ($41.5M) creates a larger equity requirement and compresses cash conversion (~60% FCF/EBITDA). The model assumes NWC stabilizes at ~18% of revenue after Year 1 optimization ($3–5M inventory reduction). If optimization does not materialize, annual FCF is reduced by $1–2M, deleveraging slows, and exit equity is lower. Working capital seasonality (February–March inventory peak of $38–40M) also creates intra-year cash flow volatility that must be managed through the revolver.

### 2. Customer Concentration

Top 5 customers represent 32% of revenue. The model assumes no customer loss over the hold period. Loss of the top customer (9% of revenue, $16.7M) would reduce EBITDA by approximately $2.5–3.0M (at blended contribution margins). This would compress returns by 0.3–0.4x MOIC. Customer retention is directly linked to the founder transition — the top 10 customer relationships are personally concentrated in Richard Hoffman.

### 3. Founder Transition

The model assumes a successful CEO transition with no revenue disruption. This is the highest-execution-risk assumption in the model. Hoffman's exit creates three simultaneous risks: (a) customer relationship transfer failure, (b) institutional knowledge loss, and (c) cultural disruption. The 12–18 month consulting agreement and structured co-management plan mitigate but do not eliminate this risk. Downside case models a scenario where transition friction leads to flat revenue and margin compression.

### 4. Chemical Handling / Environmental Regulatory

Environmental compliance costs are currently $0.8M annually with no pending regulatory actions. The model does not include a reserve for environmental remediation. Phase I/II environmental site assessments during DD will confirm or flag exposure. A material environmental finding at the Cincinnati HQ (150,000 sq ft chemical storage, 40 years of operations) could create an unbudgeted liability of $2–5M+ and delay closing.

### 5. Raw Material Cost Volatility

SCS passes through raw material cost increases with a 60–90 day lag. In a rising cost environment, gross margins compress temporarily by up to 100bps. The model assumes stable input costs; a sustained inflationary cycle could compress margins in Years 1–2 before pricing catches up.
