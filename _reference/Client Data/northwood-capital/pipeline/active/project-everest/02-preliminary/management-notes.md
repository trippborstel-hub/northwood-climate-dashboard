## Session 1: Richard Hoffman — CEO & Founder

**Duration:** 3 hours (individual session)

### Background & Story

Hoffman founded SCS in 1985 at age 22 after working for three years as a technical sales engineer for a major chemical manufacturer. He saw that mid-market industrial customers were underserved — national distributors treated them as low-priority accounts, and local distributors lacked technical expertise. He started with water treatment chemicals for industrial cooling systems, making deliveries himself from a rented warehouse in Cincinnati.

Hoffman is now 63 and has run this business for 40 years. He has no children in the business and no identified successor. He is physically and mentally sharp but is clear that he wants to retire within 12 months. His wife's health condition (not elaborated) is a secondary motivator for the timeline. He is not looking to roll equity or stay in any operational capacity — this is a clean exit.

### Impressions

**Strengths:**
- **Institutional knowledge that cannot be documented.** Hoffman knows every major customer by name, knows their production processes, knows which chemicals they use and why, and knows the competitive landscape at a granular level. This is 40 years of accumulated market intelligence residing in one person's head.
- **Genuine care for employees and customers.** Hoffman mentioned employees and customers more than financial returns. He has built a culture of loyalty — average employee tenure is 12 years, which is exceptional for a distribution business. Several employees have been with the Company for 20+ years.
- **Prudent financial management.** No debt. No outside capital. Consistent investment in facilities and fleet. The business has been managed conservatively and profitably for four decades. Hoffman is not a financial engineer — he is a builder and operator.
- **Honest broker.** Hoffman was transparent about the business's challenges: margin pressure from raw material cost volatility, difficulty recruiting younger workers into chemical distribution, and the concentration of customer relationships in himself. This candor is unusual in a sales process and builds credibility.

**Concerns:**
- **The succession problem is severe.** Hoffman does not have a successor. He has not groomed one. There is no COO, no President, no "number two" who can step into the CEO role. Schmidt (CFO) and Walsh (VP Sales) are strong functional leaders but neither has the breadth or the aspiration to be CEO. Hoffman acknowledges this openly: "I should have started planning this five years ago."
- **Customer relationships are concentrated in Hoffman.** The top 10 customers all have direct, personal relationships with Hoffman. He visits them regularly, attends their industry events, and is often the first call when there is a problem. When asked who would own these relationships post-exit, Hoffman paused and said: "Karen knows them all, but they call me." This is not a transition — it is a cliff.
- **Limited formal systems.** Despite the SAP Business One ERP implementation, many critical processes still run through Hoffman's judgment. Pricing decisions, credit approvals above $50K, new product decisions, and supplier negotiations all funnel through him. The management team executes well but within a framework that Hoffman sets informally.
- **Timeline pressure.** Hoffman wants to close and exit within 6-9 months. This creates urgency that could work in our favor (motivated seller) but also creates execution risk (compressed transition window).

### Key Quotes

- On succession: "I should have hired a CEO five years ago. That is my one regret. But I kept thinking I had more time."
- On customers: "In this business, people buy from people. Our customers have been buying from me for 20 years. That trust takes time to transfer."
- On the business: "Specialty chemicals are boring. That is why we make money. Nobody disrupts boring."
- On his team: "David [Schmidt] could run the numbers for any business in America. Karen [Walsh] knows how to sell. They need a leader, not a manager."

## Session 2: David Schmidt — CFO

**Duration:** 2 hours (individual session)

### Background

Schmidt joined SCS in 2007 as Controller and was promoted to CFO in 2012. CPA (Ohio). Previously spent 6 years in public accounting (BKD, now Forvis Mazars) in the manufacturing and distribution practice. He has been the financial backbone of the Company for 18 years — managing all financial reporting, banking relationships, insurance, and regulatory compliance.

### Impressions

**Strengths:**
- **Deep institutional knowledge of the financials.** Schmidt knows every line item, every cost driver, every seasonal pattern. He can explain why March gross margins are 50bps below May, why Q4 inventory builds $3M, and why DSO spikes in July (agricultural customers on extended payment terms). This is not theoretical — it is operational financial intelligence built over 18 years.
- **Credible numbers.** The financial presentation was clean, well-documented, and conservative. Schmidt does not inflate or spin. The EBITDA adjustments are straightforward: $1.0M owner comp normalization (Hoffman pays himself above-market), $0.2M owner perquisites, $0.3M Louisville warehouse buildout costs. All defensible.
- **Strong banking relationships.** SCS has no debt but maintains a $10M revolving credit facility with Fifth Third Bank (undrawn) and has a long-standing relationship. Schmidt has also managed the Company's insurance program (property, liability, environmental, fleet, workers comp) effectively — loss ratios are well below industry average.
- **Transparent about challenges.** Schmidt flagged the working capital intensity proactively. He walked through the inventory build rationale (safety stock, regulatory, customer service commitment) and identified $3-5M in optimization opportunity. He also flagged raw material cost volatility as the primary margin risk — SCS passes through price increases but with a 60-90 day lag.

**Concerns:**
- **No PE experience.** Like many long-tenure CFOs at founder-led businesses, Schmidt has never operated in a PE-backed environment. He will need support on board reporting, covenant compliance, and the speed and depth of financial reporting that a PE sponsor requires. The foundation is strong — the gap is pace and format, not capability.
- **Not a CEO candidate.** Schmidt is a classic CFO — detail-oriented, risk-aware, operationally sound. He does not have the commercial vision or the leadership presence to run the company. He knows this and is comfortable in the CFO role. This is a positive — he is not angling for a promotion he is not suited for.

### Financial Items Discussed

- Working capital seasonality: Inventory peaks in February-March ($38-40M) ahead of spring agricultural season and troughs in October-November ($32-33M). AR peaks in July-August (agricultural payment terms) and normalizes by October.
- Gross margin by product: Custom blends 32-35%, water treatment 27-28%, metalworking 24-26%, food processing 25-27%, institutional 23-25%, agricultural 20-22%.
- Raw material cost pass-through: 60-90 day lag. In a rising cost environment, margins compress temporarily but recover. In a declining cost environment, margins benefit temporarily. Historical volatility adds +/- 100bps to annual gross margin.
- Fleet costs: $4.2M annually (fuel, maintenance, insurance, depreciation). 42 owned trucks. Average age: 5 years. Fleet is well-maintained — no deferred capex.
- Environmental compliance costs: $0.8M annually (hazmat storage, waste disposal, regulatory reporting, facility inspections). No pending EPA actions. No known environmental liabilities. Phase I/II recommended during DD.

## Session 3: Karen Walsh — VP of Sales

**Duration:** 1.5 hours (individual session)

### Background

Walsh joined SCS in 2017 after 15 years in sales leadership at a national specialty chemical distributor (Brenntag). She was hired by Hoffman specifically to professionalize the sales organization — which at the time had 18 sales reps with no formal territories, no CRM, no sales process, and no performance management.

### Impressions

**Strengths:**
- **Professional sales leader.** Walsh transformed the commercial organization. She implemented Salesforce CRM, created defined territories, established formal sales targets and compensation plans, and hired 7 additional reps. The sales team is now 25 people organized into 4 regional territories with quarterly business reviews and pipeline tracking.
- **Knows the customer base cold.** Walsh can articulate the value proposition for each end market, the competitive dynamics in each geography, and the expansion opportunities by territory. She has personally visited every top-50 customer at least quarterly since joining.
- **Growth-oriented.** Walsh is the closest thing to a commercial strategist in the organization. She has identified 3 new end markets (semiconductor manufacturing, pharmaceutical, cannabis/hemp processing) that represent $15-20M in addressable revenue within the Company's existing geography. She also identified the 5 add-on acquisition targets and has preliminary relationships with 2 of the founders.
- **Potential internal leader.** Of the three management team members, Walsh is the most likely to step into a broader leadership role under new ownership. She has the ambition, the commercial acumen, and the people skills. She is not a CEO candidate today (lacks P&L ownership experience and operational breadth) but could be developed toward a Chief Commercial Officer or Chief Revenue Officer role.

**Concerns:**
- **Customer relationship gap.** Despite 9 years at SCS, Walsh acknowledges that the top 5 customers still prefer Hoffman as their primary contact. She has built relationships with the operating-level contacts (plant managers, procurement directors) but the C-suite relationships belong to Hoffman. The transition will require deliberate, structured co-management over 6-12 months.
- **Sales team quality is uneven.** Walsh rates 15 of her 25 reps as "strong" or "excellent," 7 as "adequate," and 3 as "below standard." The bottom 3 are legacy reps from the pre-Walsh era who are protected by Hoffman's loyalty. Walsh has wanted to replace them for years but Hoffman will not allow it. Post-close, this should be addressed quickly.

### Competitive Intelligence

- The two most dangerous competitors in the Ohio Valley are a Cleveland-based industrial chemical distributor (Target A in the CIM) and a new entrant backed by a national chemical manufacturer's distribution arm. The national entrant is competing on price and is not sustainable at current levels — but it creates pricing pressure in the metalworking segment.
- Amazon / e-commerce disruption is not a concern for specialty chemicals. "Nobody is ordering custom-blended metalworking fluid from Amazon. Our customers need technical support, compliance documentation, and same-day delivery. You cannot disintermediate that with a website."

## Facility Tour & General Observations

### Custom Blending Facility

- 25,000 sq ft purpose-built blending facility with 8 mixing tanks (500-5,000 gallon capacity)
- Batch process with full quality control lab on-site
- Clean, well-organized, appropriate safety signage and PPE compliance
- 3 full-time chemists and 8 blending operators
- Facility operates at approximately 70% capacity — room for growth without capital investment
- Product testing lab is equipped with standard analytical instruments (viscometers, pH meters, titration equipment)

### Main Warehouse & Distribution

- 150,000 sq ft facility — approximately 80% utilized
- Segregated storage for hazardous, flammable, and food-grade chemicals (regulatory requirement)
- Modern warehouse management system integrated with SAP
- Fleet yard: 42 trucks, clean and well-maintained, average age 5 years
- Loading dock operations appeared efficient — observed 6 trucks being loaded simultaneously during the tour

### General Observations

- Facility is clean, well-maintained, and professionally operated. No deferred maintenance visible.
- Employees were engaged and professional. Several recognized Hoffman by name as we walked through — strong cultural indicators.
- Environmental controls appeared sound: secondary containment around all storage areas, spill response equipment visible, safety shower stations at appropriate intervals.
- One concern: some sections of the main warehouse have older roof sections that may require replacement within 3-5 years. Schmidt flagged this — estimated cost $1.5-2.0M.

## Key Takeaways & Action Items

### Overall Assessment

The business is strong. The management team is competent but structured around Hoffman. The single most important issue in this deal is the CEO succession — everything else is manageable.

### Founder Transition — Critical Path

This is the defining execution challenge. Unlike Project Orion (where the founder is rolling equity and staying), Hoffman is making a clean exit. The business has operated for 40 years with one leader, and that leader will be gone within 6-9 months of close. This requires:

1. **External CEO hire — pre-close or within 30 days of close.** This is non-negotiable. The CEO search should begin immediately upon exclusivity. Target profile: distribution/industrial executive, 20+ years experience, prior PE-backed CEO role preferred, Ohio/Midwest based or willing to relocate.
2. **Hoffman transition advisory agreement.** Even though Hoffman wants a clean exit, he must agree to a 6-12 month transition advisory role (part-time, 2-3 days per month) focused exclusively on customer relationship introductions and institutional knowledge transfer. Compensated separately from the transaction proceeds.
3. **Customer communication plan.** Top 20 customers must receive personal communication about the transition before or at close. Joint meetings (Hoffman + new CEO + Walsh) for the top 10 accounts within the first 30 days.
4. **Operating Partner engagement.** David Okafor (Northwood Operating Partner, industrial focus) should be assigned to this portfolio company. His 25 years of industrial operations experience and prior CEO background are directly relevant.

### Key Hires Post-Close

1. **CEO** (external recruitment, begin during DD period)
2. **VP of Corporate Development** (to execute add-on acquisition strategy)
3. **Director of Supply Chain** (to optimize the $35M+ inventory position)

### Retention Priorities

1. David Schmidt (CFO) — equity participation, stable role, PE mentorship
2. Karen Walsh (VP Sales) — equity participation, expanded role (Chief Commercial Officer potential), sales team autonomy
3. Robert Chen (VP Operations) — equity participation, operational continuity
