**Northwood Capital Partners**

## Document Standards

| Parameter | Specification |
|-----------|--------------|
| Target length | 25-35 pages (excluding appendices) |
| Format | Markdown or PDF, 11pt body, single-spaced |
| Distribution | IC members only (Managing Partners, Partners) |
| Lead time | Circulated 5 business days prior to IC meeting |
| Financial basis | Adjusted EBITDA (see Conventions below) |
| Valuation method | EV / Adjusted EBITDA |
| EBITDA convention | Adjusted (normalize for one-time items, owner compensation, run-rate synergies with clear bridge) |
| Leverage basis | Total Debt / Adjusted EBITDA |
| Returns | Gross IRR/MOIC at portco level; net IRR/MOIC at fund level |
| Rounding | Currency: $X.XM; Multiples: X.Xx; Margins: X.X%; IRR: integer with ~ if approximate |

## Section 1: Executive Summary

**Purpose:** Provide the IC with a complete decision-ready overview on a single page. A committee member who reads only this page should understand what we are buying, why, at what price, and what the deal team recommends.

**Target length:** 1 page (strictly enforced)

**Required data points:**

- Company name, headquarters, founding year
- Sector and subsector classification
- LTM revenue and Adjusted EBITDA (with margin)
- Projected revenue and Adjusted EBITDA (with margin)
- Enterprise value range and implied EV/EBITDA multiple range
- Equity check (Northwood share at 80% fund / 20% management rollover)
- Entry leverage (Total Debt / Adjusted EBITDA)
- Investment thesis in 2-3 sentences
- Base case returns: IRR and MOIC
- Deal lead and team
- Process type and competitive dynamics
- Seller rationale
- Fund and remaining committed capital / deployment capacity
- Recommendation with conditions (if any)

**Format:**

```
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Company:         [Name]
Headquarters:    [City, State]
Sector:          [Sector / Subsector]
Founded:         [Year]

LTM Revenue:     $[X.X]M
LTM Adj. EBITDA: $[X.X]M ([X.X]% margin)
Proj. Revenue:   $[X.X]M
Proj. EBITDA:    $[X.X]M ([X.X]% margin)

Enterprise Value:     $[Low]M - $[High]M ([X.X]x - [X.X]x LTM Adj. EBITDA)
Total Equity:         $[X.X]M
  Fund Equity (80%):  $[X.X]M
  Mgmt Rollover (20%):$[X.X]M
Entry Leverage:       [X.X]x

Base Case Returns:    [XX]% IRR / [X.X]x MOIC over [X]-year hold

Thesis: [2-3 sentence summary of the core investment thesis]

Recommendation: [Proceed / Pass / Defer] — [1 sentence condition or rationale]
```

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Exceeding one page. If it does not fit, the thesis is not clear enough.
- Burying the recommendation. Lead with the ask.
- Using projected EBITDA as the valuation basis without flagging it. Always anchor to LTM and show the bridge.
- Omitting remaining fund capacity. The IC needs deployment context.
- Stating thesis without the "why we're right" — what is the differentiated insight?

## Section 2: Company Overview

**Purpose:** Give the IC enough context to understand the business model, history, competitive position, and management quality. This section establishes whether the company is the type of business Northwood acquires.

**Target length:** 3-4 pages

**Required data points:**

- Company history and founding narrative
- Business model description (how the company makes money)
- Products and/or services breakdown with revenue attribution
- Revenue model (recurring vs. project, contract terms, renewal rates)
- Customer base (count, type, concentration, top 10 with revenue %)
- Geographic footprint (locations, regional vs. national, expansion trajectory)
- Employee count and organizational structure
- Management team bios (CEO, CFO, COO at minimum)
  - Tenure, background, prior PE experience (if any)
  - Assessment of whether team can scale with the platform
  - Identified gaps and planned hires
- Key contracts, licenses, or certifications
- Technology and systems overview

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Regurgitating the CIM. Rewrite in Northwood's analytical voice.
- Skipping the revenue model mechanics. "Business services company" is not a business model description. Explain bill rate vs. pay rate, contract structure, margin drivers.
- Describing management without assessing them. The IC wants to know: can this team execute the value creation plan? Where are the gaps?
- Omitting customer concentration analysis. Always compute top 1, top 5, and top 10 customer revenue percentages. Check for hidden concentration (e.g., related-entity customers).

## Section 3: Market & Competitive Landscape

**Purpose:** Establish the market context — size, growth, structure, and competitive dynamics — that supports or undermines the investment thesis. The IC needs to understand whether this is a good market to invest in, and whether this company is well-positioned within it.

**Target length:** 2-3 pages

**Required data points:**

- Total Addressable Market (TAM) with source and methodology
- Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM) — the subset Northwood actually underwrites
- Market growth rate (historical and projected, with drivers)
- Market structure: fragmented vs. consolidated, number of participants, size distribution
- Key secular growth drivers (regulatory, demographic, technology, macro)
- Key secular headwinds or disruption risks
- Competitive landscape
  - Direct competitors (3-5) with estimated revenue, differentiation
  - Indirect competitors or substitutes
  - Barriers to entry (licensing, capital, relationships, scale, certification)
- Company's competitive positioning
  - Market share (if estimable)
  - Differentiation and defensibility
  - Win rate or competitive displacement data (if available)
- Consolidation activity: recent M&A transactions, buyer profiles, multiples paid

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Citing TAM figures without scrutinizing them. CIM TAMs are routinely inflated. Triangulate with independent sources.
- Ignoring disruption risk. Every market has substitution threats. Name them and assess probability.
- Listing competitors without comparative analysis. The IC needs to understand relative positioning, not just names.
- Failing to connect market dynamics to thesis. If the thesis depends on market fragmentation, prove the fragmentation with data.

## Section 4: Financial Analysis

**Purpose:** Present a rigorous analysis of historical financial performance that establishes trend lines, validates the EBITDA bridge, and identifies working capital and capex dynamics. This section must give the IC confidence in the quality of earnings.

**Target length:** 4-5 pages (plus appendix tables)

**Required data points:**

- Historical P&L summary (3-5 years)
  - Revenue, gross profit, EBITDA, adjusted EBITDA
  - Year-over-year growth rates
  - Gross margin, EBITDA margin, adjusted EBITDA margin
- EBITDA bridge: reported to adjusted, with each adjustment itemized and justified
- Revenue mix analysis
  - By product/service line
  - By customer segment or vertical
  - By geography
  - Recurring vs. non-recurring
- Gross margin drivers and trends
  - Input cost trends (labor, materials)
  - Pricing power / bill rate increases vs. cost increases
- SG&A analysis (fixed vs. variable, key cost centers)
- Working capital analysis
  - DSO, DPO, DIO trends
  - Net working capital as % of revenue
  - Seasonal working capital swings
- Capital expenditure requirements
  - Maintenance capex vs. growth capex (separate clearly)
  - Capex as % of revenue (historical and projected)
- Free cash flow conversion
  - Adjusted EBITDA to unlevered free cash flow bridge
  - Cash conversion ratio (FCF / Adjusted EBITDA)
- Quality of Earnings assessment
  - QofE provider and scope (if engaged)
  - Key findings and adjustments
  - Remaining open items

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Accepting EBITDA adjustments at face value. Every adjustment over $250K needs independent validation. "One-time" items that recur every year are not adjustments.
- Conflating maintenance and growth capex. If the company has been underinvesting in maintenance, the true run-rate EBITDA is lower than reported.
- Ignoring working capital in the returns model. A business with $35M+ in inventory has a different cash profile than one with negative working capital.
- Presenting margins without explaining drivers. If margins expanded 300bps, what drove it? Price increases, mix shift, cost reductions, or accounting changes?
- Missing seasonality. Revenue and working capital patterns matter for the financing structure.

## Section 5: Investment Thesis

**Purpose:** Articulate the 3-5 core beliefs that underpin the investment. Each thesis pillar should state what we believe, present the evidence, explain why we are right (or differently positioned to win), and describe how it creates value. This is the intellectual core of the memo.

**Target length:** 2-3 pages

**Required structure (per thesis pillar):**

```
### Thesis Pillar [#]: [Title]

**What we believe:** [1-2 sentence statement of the thesis element]

**Evidence:**
- [Data point or observation supporting the belief]
- [Data point or observation supporting the belief]
- [Data point or observation supporting the belief]

**Why we're right:** [1-2 sentences on differentiated insight, proprietary angle, or
analytical edge. What do we see that the market does not?]

**Value creation mechanism:** [How this thesis pillar translates to EBITDA growth,
multiple expansion, or risk reduction]

**Key assumption to monitor:** [What must remain true for this pillar to hold?]
```

**Typical thesis pillar categories for Northwood deals:**

- Organic growth acceleration (new markets, products, channels)
- Add-on acquisition platform (fragmented market, identified targets, arbitrage)
- Operational improvement (margin expansion, cost reduction, efficiency)
- Management upgrade or reinforcement
- Secular demand tailwind (regulatory, demographic, technology)

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Stating the thesis without the "why we're right." Every PE firm can write "fragmented market." What is Northwood's differentiated angle?
- Conflating growth with value creation. Revenue growth matters only if it flows to EBITDA and cash. Show the margin bridge.
- Creating thesis pillars that are not independently testable. If a pillar cannot be validated or invalidated with specific data, it is an assertion, not a thesis.
- Having more than 5 pillars. If you have 7, you do not have a thesis — you have a list.

## Section 6: Value Creation Plan

**Purpose:** Lay out the concrete operational and strategic actions the deal team and Operating Partners will execute post-close to realize the investment thesis. Each initiative should have an owner, a timeline, and a quantified impact on EBITDA.

**Target length:** 3-4 pages

**Required data points:**

- **100-Day Plan priorities** (3-5 critical actions in first 100 days post-close)
  - Quick wins with measurable impact
  - Management and organizational decisions
  - Key hires
  - Systems and reporting upgrades

- **Operational improvements** (12-36 month horizon)
  - Margin expansion initiatives with quantified EBITDA impact
  - Procurement / vendor consolidation
  - Pricing optimization
  - SG&A rationalization
  - Technology and automation investments
  - Working capital improvement

- **Add-on acquisition strategy**
  - Number and type of targets identified
  - Target size range (revenue, EBITDA)
  - Expected acquisition multiples (vs. platform entry multiple — document the arbitrage)
  - Geographic or capability expansion logic
  - Integration playbook readiness
  - Financing approach (revolver draws, incremental term loan, equity co-invest)

- **Management actions**
  - Organizational chart changes
  - Incentive alignment (management equity plan, KPI-linked bonuses)
  - Board composition and cadence
  - Operating Partner assignments (reference Northwood operating team)

- **Value creation bridge**
  - Entry EBITDA to exit EBITDA: organic growth + margin improvement + add-on contribution
  - Entry multiple to exit multiple: what drives expansion (scale, recurring revenue, diversification)?
  - Total value creation attribution: EBITDA growth vs. multiple expansion vs. leverage/FCF

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Value creation plans that depend entirely on revenue growth. The IC wants to see operational levers.
- Add-on strategies without specific targets. "We'll do add-ons" is not a strategy. Name the targets, the owners, the geography, and the expected timeline.
- Omitting the operating partner role. Northwood's operating partners (David Okafor for industrial, Lisa Brennan for healthcare) should have defined mandates.
- Failing to quantify. Every initiative needs an estimated EBITDA impact range and a timeline. "Improve procurement" is not actionable. "$1.5-2.0M annual savings from vendor consolidation by Month 18" is.

## Section 7: Transaction Summary

**Purpose:** Present the full transaction structure including sources and uses, capital structure, and key terms. The IC needs to understand how the deal is financed, what the equity exposure is, and whether the leverage is appropriate.

**Target length:** 2-3 pages

**Required data points:**

- **Sources & Uses table**

```
SOURCES                              USES
Senior Term Loan    $[X.X]M          Enterprise Value      $[X.X]M
Revolving Credit    $[X.X]M          Financing Fees        $[X.X]M
  Total Debt        $[X.X]M          Transaction Costs     $[X.X]M
Fund Equity (80%)   $[X.X]M          Working Capital Adj.  $[X.X]M
Mgmt Rollover (20%) $[X.X]M
  Total Equity      $[X.X]M
-----------                          -----------
Total Sources       $[X.X]M          Total Uses            $[X.X]M
```

- **Capital structure details**
  - Lender(s) and relationship context
  - Term loan: amount, spread (SOFR +), maturity, amortization
  - Revolver: committed amount, drawn at close, availability
  - Total leverage at close (Total Debt / Adjusted EBITDA)
  - Senior leverage at close
  - Interest coverage ratio
  - Key covenants (leverage, fixed charge coverage, capex limits)
  - Financing fees and OID

- **Equity structure**
  - Total equity check
  - Fund equity (80% per convention)
  - Management rollover (20% per convention)
  - Co-invest allocation (if any, with LP co-invest commitments)
  - Management equity incentive pool (% of fully diluted equity, vesting, triggers)

- **Key transaction terms**
  - Purchase agreement structure (stock vs. asset, tax implications)
  - Representations and warranties
  - Indemnification (escrow amount and term, basket, cap)
  - Non-compete / non-solicit provisions
  - Earnout or contingent consideration (if any)
  - Closing conditions

- **Transaction timeline**
  - LOI/exclusivity date
  - Due diligence period
  - Financing commitment date
  - Expected close date

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Sources and uses that do not balance. Always foot the table.
- Omitting financing fees and transaction costs from the uses side. These are real cash outflows.
- Presenting leverage without context. State the leverage relative to Northwood's target range (typically 3.0-4.0x for Fund I/II deals) and relative to sector benchmarks.
- Skipping covenant analysis. If the deal is leveraged 3.5x and the covenant trips at 4.5x, what is the cushion, and what EBITDA decline triggers a breach?

## Section 8: Returns Analysis

**Purpose:** Present the financial return expectations under multiple scenarios. The IC must understand the range of outcomes, the key sensitivities, and the risk/return tradeoff.

**Target length:** 2-3 pages (plus appendix model output)

**Required data points:**

- **Three-case framework:**

| Metric | Downside | Base | Upside |
|--------|----------|------|--------|
| Revenue CAGR | | | |
| Exit EBITDA | | | |
| Exit margin | | | |
| Exit multiple | | | |
| Implied exit EV | | | |
| Net debt at exit | | | |
| Equity value | | | |
| Gross IRR | | | |
| Gross MOIC | | | |
| Hold period (years) | | | |

- **Case assumptions** (for each case)
  - Revenue growth trajectory
  - Margin expansion / contraction
  - Add-on acquisitions included (number, size, cost)
  - Debt paydown from FCF
  - Exit multiple assumption and rationale
  - Hold period

- **Sensitivity analysis**
  - IRR sensitivity to exit multiple (7x-12x range, 0.5x increments)
  - IRR sensitivity to exit year (Year 3-7)
  - IRR sensitivity to EBITDA at exit (vary +/- 20%)
  - Two-way table: exit multiple x exit EBITDA

- **Returns attribution**
  - EBITDA growth contribution to MOIC
  - Multiple expansion/contraction contribution to MOIC
  - Leverage/FCF contribution to MOIC (debt paydown + distributions)

- **Comparable exit transactions**
  - 3-5 recent transactions in the sector
  - Buyer type (strategic vs. sponsor), EV, EBITDA multiple, deal context

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Base case that is actually the upside. The base case should reflect plan achievement, not perfection. If the base case requires every thesis pillar to work, it is not a base case.
- Downside case that is not painful enough. The downside should model a realistic adverse scenario — recession, customer loss, thesis failure — not "slightly below plan."
- Returns that depend on multiple expansion. Northwood's underwriting standard is: base case returns must work at flat entry/exit multiples. Multiple expansion is upside, not underwriting.
- Ignoring timing. A 2.5x MOIC over 7 years is ~14% IRR. The same MOIC over 4 years is ~26% IRR. Hold period matters.
- Omitting comparable exit data. The IC needs evidence that the assumed exit multiple is achievable.

## Section 9: Key Risks & Mitigants

**Purpose:** Identify the 5-7 most significant risks to the investment and present specific, credible mitigants. This section demonstrates that the deal team has stress-tested the thesis and has a plan for adverse scenarios.

**Target length:** 2-3 pages

**Required structure (per risk):**

```
### Risk [#]: [Risk Title]

**Description:** [1-2 sentences describing the risk and how it manifests]

**Severity:** [Low / Medium / High / Critical]

**Likelihood:** [Remote / Possible / Likely / Almost Certain]

**Priority:** [Derived from severity x likelihood per risk framework]

**Mitigant(s):**
- [Specific action, contractual protection, or structural feature that reduces this risk]
- [Specific action, contractual protection, or structural feature that reduces this risk]

**Residual risk assessment:** [After mitigants, what is the remaining exposure?
Quantify where possible — e.g., "If top customer churns, EBITDA impact is $X.XM
representing X% of total. Replacement timeline estimated at 12-18 months."]

**Monitoring trigger:** [What observable event or metric deterioration would signal
this risk is materializing?]
```

**Standard risk categories to evaluate:**

1. Market / industry risk (cyclicality, secular decline, disruption)
2. Customer concentration risk (top 1/5/10 analysis, hidden concentration)
3. Management / key person risk (founder dependency, team gaps)
4. Operational risk (execution complexity, integration, scaling)
5. Financial risk (leverage, covenant headroom, working capital)
6. Regulatory / legal risk (compliance burden, pending regulation, litigation)
7. Technology risk (platform obsolescence, migration, cybersecurity)
8. ESG risk (environmental liability, labor practices, governance)

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Generic risks without specific mitigants. "The economy could slow down" is not risk analysis. "A recession reduces client spending on temp staffing 15-20%, compressing EBITDA by $3-4M. Mitigant: variable cost structure (70% of COGS is contingent labor) enables rapid cost adjustment within 30-60 days" is risk analysis.
- Omitting hidden concentration. Check whether nominally different customers share common ownership. CIMs routinely present subsidiary relationships as independent customers.
- Listing mitigants you do not control. "Management will retain key employees" is not a mitigant unless you have retention agreements with defined terms.
- Underweighting founder/CEO transition risk. In every management buyout with a departing founder, quantify the customer relationships, institutional knowledge, and cultural leadership at risk.

## Section 10: Due Diligence Status

**Purpose:** Report the current status of all due diligence workstreams so the IC can assess whether sufficient work has been completed to support the recommendation.

**Target length:** 1-2 pages

**Required data points:**

- **Completed workstreams** (with provider and key findings)
  - Quality of Earnings (QofE)
  - Legal due diligence
  - Tax due diligence
  - Insurance review
  - Environmental assessment (if applicable)
  - IT / cybersecurity assessment
  - Market study / commercial due diligence
  - Management references
  - Background checks
  - Customer calls (number completed, key takeaways)
  - Employee / culture assessment

- **In-progress workstreams** (with expected completion date)

- **Open items and unresolved findings**
  - List each open item with owner and target resolution date
  - Indicate which open items are "must resolve before close" vs. "post-close"

- **Timeline to close**

```
Milestone                  Target Date    Status
LOI / Exclusivity          [Date]         [Complete / Pending]
Quality of Earnings        [Date]         [Complete / In Progress]
Legal DD                   [Date]         [Complete / In Progress]
Financing Commitment       [Date]         [Pending]
Purchase Agreement Draft   [Date]         [Pending]
IC Final Approval          [Date]         [Pending]
Signing                    [Date]         [Pending]
Closing                    [Date]         [Pending]
```

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Recommending approval with material DD workstreams incomplete. If QofE is not done, the IC cannot approve a final bid.
- Listing "customer calls" without synthesizing findings. Report the call count, coverage (% of revenue represented), and key themes — not just "5 calls completed."
- Omitting the cost of DD. If Northwood has spent $500K on advisors and the deal falls apart, that is a real cost. Report cumulative DD spend.

## Section 11: Recommendation

**Purpose:** State the deal team's recommendation clearly and without ambiguity. Summarize the key arguments for and against, and specify any conditions on the recommendation.

**Target length:** 1 page

**Required structure:**

```
RECOMMENDATION: [PROCEED / PASS / DEFER]

Deal Lead: [Name]
IC Meeting Date: [Date]
Fund: Northwood Capital Fund [I/II], L.P.
```

**If PROCEED:**
- Restate the core thesis in 2-3 sentences
- Confirm that base case returns meet fund hurdles (target: 20%+ gross IRR, 2.5x+ gross MOIC)
- List any conditions on the approval (e.g., "Subject to QofE confirmation of Adjusted EBITDA within 10% of underwritten," "Subject to financing commitment at terms no worse than SOFR+500/6yr")
- Identify the 1-2 risks the team is most concerned about and why they are acceptable
- State the next action (submit bid, proceed to confirmatory DD, negotiate purchase agreement)

**If PASS:**
- State the primary reason concisely (valuation, risk profile, fit, quality)
- Note whether the opportunity should be monitored for re-engagement
- Specify the trigger for re-engagement (e.g., "Re-evaluate if process stalls and seller expectations moderate to 9-10x")
- Acknowledge what was attractive about the opportunity

**If DEFER:**
- State what information or event is needed before a decision can be made
- Set a specific date for the follow-up IC discussion
- Assign action items with owners and deadlines

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Ambiguous recommendations. "We like the business but have concerns" is not a recommendation. Commit to Proceed, Pass, or Defer.
- Recommendations without conditions. Almost every Proceed comes with conditions. State them explicitly.
- Passing without documenting the logic. If we re-encounter this company or sector, the pass memo is institutional memory. Write it for the team that evaluates the next deal in this space.
