# Due Diligence Workplan Methodology

*Northwood Capital | Chicago, IL*
*Reference Version: February 2026*

---

## 1. Purpose and Scope

This document encodes Northwood Capital's methodology for executing due diligence from LOI execution through close. It is an operational reference — not a policy document. It defines how diligence is structured, assigned, sequenced, and governed so that any deal team member can execute a full DD process that meets Northwood's institutional standard.

**This document applies to all platform acquisitions** beginning at LOI execution. It does not govern preliminary DD (pre-LOI financial review and management assessment), which is handled through the screening and IC memo process. Add-on acquisitions follow an abbreviated version of this process defined separately in the add-on diligence protocol.

**What this document governs:**

- Workstream architecture — the full set of diligence workstreams, their owners, and their mandates
- Sequencing — what runs in parallel, what is gated, and why
- Vendor management — when advisors are engaged, what they are scoped to deliver, and how findings are integrated
- Governance — weekly DD cadence, escalation protocols, IC interface, and data room management
- Output package — what the full diligence output looks like when it reaches IC

**What this document does not govern:**

- Financing execution (syndication, commitment letters, credit agreement negotiation) — handled by the deal lead in coordination with the CFO
- Portfolio integration and 100-day planning — handled post-close by the deal lead and relevant Operating Partner
- Add-on acquisitions — abbreviated diligence, separate protocol

## 2. Overall DD Timeline

Standard platform diligence runs **60–90 days from LOI execution to close**. The midpoint assumption in model planning is 75 days. Auction processes with seller-imposed deadlines compress toward 60 days. Proprietary and complex deals (regulatory-intensive healthcare, multi-site industrial, contested management situations) may extend to 90 days with IC approval.

The timeline below represents the standard 75-day process. Deal leads adapt this to specific process dynamics at kickoff.

| Milestone | Owner | Target Day | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| LOI executed, exclusivity granted | James Harrison / Catherine Zhao | Day 0 | Signed LOI, exclusivity letter |
| Deal kickoff — internal | Deal Lead | Day 1 | Workplan distributed, advisors notified |
| DD kickoff with management | Deal Lead | Day 2–3 | DD request list delivered, data room access confirmed |
| QofE advisor engaged | Deal Lead + Brian Taylor (CFO) | Day 1–3 | Engagement letter signed, kickoff scheduled |
| Legal advisor engaged | Deal Lead | Day 1–3 | Engagement letter signed, scope agreed |
| All workstream advisors engaged | Deal Lead | Day 5 | Full advisor roster confirmed |
| Data room substantially complete | Management / Sell-side | Day 10 | Data room index reviewed, gap list issued |
| Management presentations (full team) | Deal Lead | Day 7–14 | Management Q&A session notes |
| Management reference calls | Deal Lead + relevant Partner | Day 10–20 | Reference call notes, key findings |
| QofE preliminary findings | QofE Advisor | Day 21 | Draft QofE deck (preliminary) |
| Legal initial findings | Legal Counsel | Day 21 | Legal diligence memo (preliminary) |
| All workstream initial findings | All workstream leads | Day 28 | Status memo per workstream |
| Full deal team synthesis meeting | Deal Lead + all workstream leads | Day 30 | Go/no-go on continuing diligence |
| DD summary circulated (draft) | Deal Lead | Day 45 | Draft DD summary memo |
| QofE final report | QofE Advisor | Day 45 | Final QofE report |
| Legal final report | Legal Counsel | Day 50 | Legal diligence memo (final) |
| All third-party reports received | All advisors | Day 50 | Full advisor report package |
| Model updated for DD findings | Thomas Chen / Rachel Kim | Day 50–55 | Updated model with DD-adjusted EBITDA |
| IC memo DD section drafted | Deal Lead | Day 55 | DD summary section + supporting memos |
| Reps and warranties agreed | Legal Counsel | Day 55–65 | Draft purchase agreement, R&W positions |
| Financing commitment received | Deal Lead | Day 60 | Commitment letter |
| IC final approval | Catherine Zhao (IC Chair) | Day 60–65 | IC authorization to close |
| Purchase agreement signed | Legal Counsel | Day 65–75 | Executed SPA |
| Close | Deal Lead + Brian Taylor | Day 75 | Wired funds, ownership transfer |

**Process note:** The Day 30 synthesis meeting is a formal go/no-go checkpoint. If diligence has surfaced findings that materially change the investment thesis or return profile, the deal team presents to IC at this point rather than continuing to spend advisor fees. This is a structural commitment, not a formality.

## 3. Workstream Architecture

Northwood runs twelve standard workstreams on every platform acquisition, plus sector-specific additions detailed in Section 4. Each workstream has a designated internal lead (by role) and an external advisor where applicable. The deal lead retains ownership of overall DD synthesis and IC deliverables.

---

### 3.1 Financial / Accounting

**Internal Lead:** Deal Lead (with Brian Taylor, CFO, in oversight role)
**External Advisor:** QofE firm (Big 4 or Grant Thornton — see Section 6)

**Key questions:**
- What is the true Adjusted EBITDA, and how does it compare to management's representation?
- What are the one-time, non-recurring, or seller-specific cost items that must be normalized?
- What are the working capital dynamics, seasonality, and cash conversion characteristics?
- Are there accounting policy differences that create comparability issues in the historical financials?
- What is the normalized capex run rate, and how does it differ from the cash flow statement?
- Are there off-balance-sheet liabilities, deferred revenue recognition issues, or revenue quality concerns?

**Primary deliverable:** Quality of Earnings report, EBITDA bridge (management claim to Northwood adjusted), normalized working capital analysis, capex analysis.

**Northwood standard:** The QofE is scoped to cover at minimum three full fiscal years plus LTM. For companies with seasonal revenue patterns, the QofE must include trailing 12-month analysis broken into quarterly periods. Every EBITDA add-back claimed by management is reviewed, classified (accepted / rejected / modified), and documented.

---

### 3.2 Commercial / Market

**Internal Lead:** Deal Lead + relevant sector Partner (Priya Kapoor for Healthcare, Mark Sullivan for Business Services, James Harrison for Industrial Technology)
**External Advisor:** Engaged for strategic market assessments on complex thesis questions; deal team leads commercial DD directly in most cases

**Key questions:**
- What is the competitive position of this business in its target market, and is it durable?
- What are the key demand drivers, and how are they expected to evolve over the hold period?
- Is the growth rate embedded in the underwrite achievable, and what are the assumptions it rests on?
- What is the customer's view of the company — do customers confirm the thesis or complicate it?
- What is the pricing power of this business, and how has it historically exercised it?
- Where are the competitive threats, and do they appear in the 5-year planning horizon?

**Primary deliverable:** Commercial DD memo (internal), customer reference call summaries, market position assessment, growth assumption validation.

**Northwood standard:** Minimum 5 customer reference calls for any deal. Customer references are conducted by the deal lead or relevant Partner — not delegated to associates. The goal is to independently validate the customer relationship thesis, not to confirm management's narrative.

---

### 3.3 Management Assessment

**Internal Lead:** Deal Lead + Managing Partner (James Harrison or Catherine Zhao)
**External Advisor:** Leadership assessment firm engaged on all transactions where founder transition or significant management rebuild is a deal thesis element

**Key questions:**
- Does the management team have the capability and capacity to execute the value creation plan?
- What is the key-person risk profile — where are the dependencies, and are they mitigable?
- Is there alignment between management's economic interests (rollover, earn-out, option pool) and Northwood's return expectations?
- Are there gaps in the management bench that need to be filled at or before close?
- What is the quality of the CFO function — can it support institutional PE reporting requirements?

**Primary deliverable:** Management assessment memo (deal lead authored), reference call summaries (minimum 3 professional references per C-suite member), compensation benchmarking vs. sector peers, proposed equity plan with alignment analysis.

**Northwood standard:** The management assessment is among the most consequential elements of diligence. We do not outsource the judgment. We use third-party assessors to add structure and rigor, but the conclusion is the deal team's. Project Orion's management assessment identified founder transition risk as a key DD focus area — James Whitfield's customer relationships and product roadmap ownership required a structured transition plan as a close condition. That kind of finding changes deal structure; it cannot be discovered after closing.

---

### 3.4 Quality of Earnings (QofE)

**Internal Lead:** Deal Lead + Brian Taylor (CFO)
**External Advisor:** Big 4 or Grant Thornton (see Section 6)

Note: QofE is listed separately from Financial / Accounting because the advisor scope and timeline are distinct, but they operate as a unified workstream. The deal lead integrates QofE findings with in-house financial analysis.

**Key questions:**
- Are the audited financials reliable? Have there been any restatements, auditor changes, or material weaknesses?
- What EBITDA figure does Northwood accept for valuation and leverage purposes?
- Are there revenue recognition practices that create timing differences or inflate LTM EBITDA?
- What is the quality of earnings — are margins sustainable or are they peak-cycle?

**Primary deliverable:** QofE report (third-party), Northwood EBITDA reconciliation table, model update memo.

---

### 3.5 Legal

**Internal Lead:** Deal Lead
**External Advisor:** Outside counsel (M&A specialist — see Section 6)

**Key questions:**
- Are there pending or threatened litigations that could result in material liability?
- Are customer contracts, supplier agreements, and key leases assignable without consent?
- Are intellectual property rights clear, properly registered, and unencumbered?
- Are there material change of control provisions in key agreements that require consents?
- What representations and warranties is the seller willing to make, and where are the gaps?
- Is R&W insurance available and appropriately sized for this transaction?

**Primary deliverable:** Legal diligence memo (by workstream: corporate, commercial contracts, IP, litigation, real estate, employment), contract consent matrix, R&W insurance recommendation.

**Northwood standard:** Northwood uses R&W insurance on all transactions unless the premium-adjusted risk profile makes it uneconomical (has not occurred in our history). The legal team is scoped to provide a consent matrix for all material contracts (>$500K annual value) within Day 21. Change-of-control provisions that require third-party consents are identified early and tracked through close.

---

### 3.6 Tax

**Internal Lead:** Brian Taylor (CFO)
**External Advisor:** Tax counsel (integrated with QofE firm or separate engagement — see Section 6)

**Key questions:**
- What is the preferred deal structure (asset vs. stock vs. 338(h)(10) election), and what are the tax consequences for each party?
- Are there federal, state, or local tax liabilities not reflected on the balance sheet (including transfer pricing, sales tax, payroll tax, and NOL utilization)?
- What is the tax basis of the target assets, and what step-up benefit is available?
- Are there tax-sharing agreements, indemnification obligations, or audit exposures from prior periods?
- What is the expected tax rate going forward, and does the model reflect it correctly?

**Primary deliverable:** Tax diligence memo, deal structure recommendation, tax basis analysis, model update for tax structure.

---

### 3.7 IT / Technology

**Internal Lead:** Deal Lead (with Operating Partner support where relevant)
**External Advisor:** IT DD specialist (see Section 6)

**Key questions:**
- Does the technology infrastructure support the business's current scale and the projected growth embedded in the thesis?
- What is the state of cybersecurity — are there known vulnerabilities, prior incidents, or compliance gaps?
- What is the technical debt burden, and what investment is required to address it?
- Are mission-critical systems proprietary or vendor-dependent? What are the renewal and migration risks?
- What is the IT capex requirement, and is it reflected in the model?
- For technology-enabled businesses: Is the product roadmap executable with the current engineering team?

**Primary deliverable:** IT / cybersecurity assessment report, system architecture overview, IT capex requirement (3-year), vendor dependency map.

**Northwood standard:** IT DD is mandatory on all transactions. The depth scales with the technology intensity of the business model. For pure-service businesses (staffing, healthcare services without proprietary tech), IT DD focuses on cybersecurity, ERP stability, and integration readiness. For technology-enabled platforms, IT DD includes architecture review, code quality assessment, and engineering team evaluation.

---

### 3.8 HR / Organizational

**Internal Lead:** Deal Lead
**External Advisor:** Engaged for large workforce transactions or where benefits liabilities are material

**Key questions:**
- What is the workforce composition (FTE vs. contractor), and are worker classification practices compliant?
- Are there ERISA liabilities (pension obligations, defined benefit plans) not reflected on the balance sheet?
- What is the turnover profile, and does it point to cultural or compensation issues?
- What are the change-of-control implications for key employee agreements (severance, non-competes, retention agreements)?
- Are non-compete and non-solicitation agreements enforceable in the relevant jurisdictions?
- What is the compensation structure vs. market benchmarks for management and key employees?

**Primary deliverable:** HR diligence memo, workforce analysis (headcount by function and FTE/contractor split), benefits liability analysis, retention risk assessment.

---

### 3.9 Insurance

**Internal Lead:** Brian Taylor (CFO)
**External Advisor:** Insurance advisor (separate from R&W carrier — see Section 6)

**Key questions:**
- Are current insurance coverages adequate for the business's risk profile and contractual requirements?
- Are there gaps in coverage that represent uninsured liabilities?
- What is the claims history, and does it indicate systemic operational risk?
- What is the D&O, GL, E&O, and workers' compensation cost baseline, and will it change post-close?
- Is R&W insurance available and properly sized for the transaction?

**Primary deliverable:** Insurance review memo, coverage gap analysis, estimated premium impact post-close, R&W insurance recommendation and sizing.

---

### 3.10 Environmental (Standard)

**Internal Lead:** Deal Lead (with David Okafor, Operating Partner, for industrial transactions)
**External Advisor:** Environmental consultant for Phase I / Phase II ESA (see Section 6)

**Key questions:**
- Are there known or suspected environmental conditions at owned or operated properties?
- What are the regulatory compliance obligations, and is the company in compliance?
- Are there historical uses of the property that create legacy liability (underground storage tanks, hazardous materials, prior industrial use)?
- What are the environmental permitting requirements for the business, and are permits current?

**Primary deliverable:** Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (mandatory for all transactions involving owned or leased real property), Phase II ESA (triggered by Phase I findings), environmental compliance review memo.

**Northwood standard:** Phase I ESA is required on all transactions regardless of sector. For industrial transactions, scope is expanded (see Section 4.3). Environmental indemnification provisions are negotiated in the purchase agreement when Phase I or Phase II findings are present.

---

### 3.11 Operations

**Internal Lead:** Relevant Operating Partner (David Okafor for Industrial Technology; Lisa Brennan for Healthcare Services)
**External Advisor:** Operational consultants engaged for large-scale operational restructuring theses; Operating Partner leads directly in most cases

**Key questions:**
- Is the operating model scalable to support the growth embedded in the thesis?
- Where are the operational bottlenecks, and what investment is required to remove them?
- What is the management team's operational reporting capability — are KPIs defined, tracked, and actioned?
- Are there cost reduction opportunities that are actionable within the first 12 months post-close?
- How does the operational model compare to Northwood's portfolio benchmarks in the sector?

**Primary deliverable:** Operations assessment memo, value creation plan input (100-day priorities), KPI baseline, cost reduction opportunity sizing.

**Northwood standard:** The relevant Operating Partner participates in all management meetings beginning at the on-site visit. They are not observers — they run the operational portion of the meeting and assess the management team directly. Their findings are incorporated into the management assessment memo. David Okafor's operational depth in industrial operations and Lisa Brennan's healthcare operations experience are core diligence assets; they should be deployed early, not as a final-stage validator.

---

### 3.12 Regulatory / Compliance (General)

**Internal Lead:** Deal Lead + Legal Counsel
**External Advisor:** Regulatory counsel (sector-specific; see Sections 4.1 and 4.3 for healthcare and industrial)

**Key questions:**
- Is the business in compliance with all material regulatory requirements?
- Are there pending regulatory changes that would materially affect the business model or cost structure?
- Are required licenses, permits, and certifications current and transferable?
- Has the company been subject to material regulatory actions, inquiries, or investigations?

**Primary deliverable:** Regulatory compliance memo, license / permit inventory with expiration dates and transfer requirements, pending regulatory risk assessment.

## 4. Sector-Specific Workstream Additions

The twelve standard workstreams apply to every deal. The following additions are mandatory based on sector. They are not optional — a Healthcare deal without a Reimbursement workstream is an incomplete DD package.

---

### 4.1 Healthcare Services Additions

Healthcare transactions require three additional workstreams beyond the standard twelve. Priya Kapoor and Lisa Brennan own these workstreams as a team.

**Reimbursement / Payor Analysis**

This is the most deal-critical workstream in healthcare DD. Reimbursement risk is the primary source of healthcare business model fragility — it has ended thesis investments post-close across the PE industry.

*Internal Lead:* Priya Kapoor (Partner), with Lisa Brennan (Operating Partner)
*External Advisor:* Healthcare reimbursement counsel or specialized healthcare DD advisor

*Key questions:*
- What is the payor mix (Medicare / Medicaid / commercial / self-pay), and how does it compare to sector benchmarks?
- What share of revenue depends on fee schedules subject to annual CMS rate updates?
- Are there pending reimbursement rate changes (Medicare Physician Fee Schedule, Medicaid state plans) that affect the business model?
- Has the company experienced any payor audits, recoupments, or demand letters? Are they resolved?
- What is the coding and billing compliance profile? Has the company had a third-party billing audit recently?
- How concentrated is the business in any single payor, and what is the contract renewal timeline?
- Is there meaningful Medicaid exposure, and if so, what is the state-level rate stability?

*Primary deliverable:* Payor mix analysis with historical trend, reimbursement rate scenario analysis (base / downside with rate compression), billing compliance review memo, payor concentration risk assessment.

*Northwood standard:* Project Horizon (Smile Partners Management, dental DSO) requires particular attention to this workstream given the dental sector's Medicaid reimbursement exposure by state. Historical reimbursement rate changes by state must be modeled explicitly.

---

**Licensing and Accreditation**

*Internal Lead:* Priya Kapoor + Legal Counsel
*External Advisor:* Healthcare regulatory counsel

*Key questions:*
- Are all required state and federal licenses current and in good standing?
- Are there accreditations (Joint Commission, CARF, AAAHC, NCQA) that are required for operations or payor contracting, and when do they expire?
- What are the change-of-control license transfer requirements in each state of operation?
- Are there any pending license renewals, deficiency findings, or corrective action plans?
- What are the de novo licensing requirements for new site development (relevant if the thesis includes organic expansion)?

*Primary deliverable:* License inventory (by entity, state, and license type) with expiration dates and renewal status, change-of-control transfer requirements matrix, accreditation status summary.

---

**HIPAA / Privacy Compliance**

*Internal Lead:* Legal Counsel + IT DD specialist
*External Advisor:* Healthcare IT and privacy counsel

*Key questions:*
- Has the company had any HIPAA breaches or OCR investigations?
- Are Business Associate Agreements current with all downstream vendors?
- Is the EHR system HIPAA-compliant and is the data security architecture adequate?
- What is the data retention policy, and does it comply with applicable requirements?

*Primary deliverable:* HIPAA compliance assessment, breach history summary, BAA inventory.

---

### 4.2 Business Services Additions

Business Services transactions require two additional workstreams: Customer Contract Analysis and Churn / Retention Analysis.

**Customer Contract Analysis**

*Internal Lead:* Deal Lead (Mark Sullivan for Business Services deals)
*External Advisor:* Legal Counsel (integrated with standard legal workstream)

*Key questions:*
- What percentage of revenue is contracted vs. at-will?
- What are the average contract terms, renewal provisions, and termination-for-convenience rights?
- Are there minimum purchase commitments or take-or-pay provisions?
- Are price escalation clauses present, and are they tied to CPI or fixed?
- What are the most common contract exceptions, and do they indicate negotiating weakness?
- Are there material customers without executed contracts (month-to-month or verbal arrangements)?

*Primary deliverable:* Contract summary matrix (by customer: revenue, term, renewal provision, termination right, escalator), revenue-at-risk analysis (contracts expiring within 12 and 24 months of close).

---

**Churn / Retention Analysis**

*Internal Lead:* Deal Lead
*External Advisor:* None — internal analysis from management's customer data

*Key questions:*
- What is the gross and net revenue retention rate for the trailing three years?
- What is the logo churn rate (count of customers lost), and how does it compare to sector benchmarks?
- Are the largest historical customer losses explainable (budget cuts, acquired by a competitor, dissatisfied) and non-systemic?
- What is the average customer tenure for the top 25 customers?
- Is the customer cohort data consistent with management's stated retention rates?

*Primary deliverable:* Cohort retention analysis (revenue-weighted, by year), customer loss log with explanations (from management, verified where possible through references), gross / net retention summary vs. sector benchmarks.

---

### 4.3 Industrial Technology Additions

Industrial Technology transactions require two additional workstreams: Capex Assessment and Environmental (Extended Scope).

**Capex Assessment**

*Internal Lead:* David Okafor (Operating Partner)
*External Advisor:* Third-party engineering firm where equipment age or condition is a thesis element

*Key questions:*
- What is the age and condition of mission-critical equipment and facilities?
- What is the maintenance capex requirement vs. management's representation in the model?
- Is there deferred maintenance — equipment that should have been replaced or overhauled but has not been?
- What is the growth capex requirement to support the revenue plan, and is it properly sized?
- Are there regulatory capital requirements (environmental controls, safety upgrades) not captured in the model?
- What is the useful life profile of major assets, and does the depreciation schedule reflect economic reality?

*Primary deliverable:* Equipment and facility condition assessment, maintenance capex vs. growth capex separation, deferred maintenance liability estimate, capex schedule for the hold period.

*Northwood standard:* David Okafor conducts on-site facility walkthroughs for all Industrial Technology deals. His 25 years of industrial operations experience — including 15 years as CEO of Precision Components Inc. — makes him more effective than a third-party assessor for identifying deferred maintenance and operational risk. Third-party engineering firms are engaged when the equipment is specialized outside his direct experience (e.g., semiconductor manufacturing equipment, advanced robotics).

---

**Environmental (Extended Scope)**

*Internal Lead:* Deal Lead + David Okafor
*External Advisor:* Environmental consultant (Phase I + Phase II + compliance review)

The standard Phase I ESA is the floor. Industrial Technology deals require:

- Phase II ESA triggered by default (not only on Phase I findings) for properties with any history of industrial use, regardless of how clean Phase I appears
- Environmental compliance audit covering air permits, wastewater discharge, hazardous materials handling and disposal, and Tier II reporting
- OSHA compliance review (integrated with the HR workstream)
- Environmental liability insurance assessment

*Key questions (beyond standard):*
- Are there environmental permits required for operations, and are they current, transferable, and properly sized for the production volume?
- Is the company in compliance with all Tier II reporting obligations?
- Are there known or suspected soil or groundwater contamination issues at any site?
- What is the estimated remediation cost if contamination is confirmed?
- Are there historical disposal practices (prior landfill use, solvent discharge) that create legacy liability?

*Primary deliverable:* Phase I and Phase II ESA reports (third-party), environmental compliance audit memo, OSHA summary, environmental remediation contingency (quantified where Phase II findings exist).

*Clearview Environmental Services (Fund II portfolio) provides a useful reference point: environmental compliance is not a bureaucratic check — it is an operational and legal liability driver that has caused material post-close surprises at peer firms. Northwood's extended scope for industrial deals reflects this.*

---

### 4.4 Certifications (Industrial Technology)

Industrial Technology deals frequently involve quality certifications that are critical to customer contracts and revenue retention.

*Internal Lead:* Deal Lead + David Okafor
*External Advisor:* Legal Counsel (integrated with standard legal workstream)

*Key questions:*
- What quality certifications does the business hold (ISO 9001, AS9100 for aerospace/defense, IATF 16949 for automotive, Nadcap for aerospace processing, SSAB for steel, CLIA for lab testing)?
- When do certifications expire, and what is the renewal process?
- Are there change-of-control requirements that could affect certification status?
- Are certifications required as a condition of any material customer contract? What is the revenue at risk if a certification lapses?
- Has the company had any certification non-conformances, corrective action requirements, or suspension risk?

*Primary deliverable:* Certification inventory (by certification, issuing body, expiration, revenue dependency), change-of-control assessment, renewal timeline.

*Pinnacle Testing & Inspection (Fund II portfolio) illustrates the stakes: its Nadcap accreditation and CLIA laboratory certification are prerequisites for its largest aerospace and government contracts. A lapse would be an immediate contract termination trigger.*

## 5. DD Governance

### 5.1 Weekly DD Team Meeting

**Format:** Weekly, 60 minutes, Tuesday 9:00 AM CT. Chaired by the deal lead. All internal workstream leads attend. External advisors attend by invitation when their workstream is the primary agenda item.

**Standard agenda:**

| Time | Agenda Item |
|---|---|
| 0:00–0:10 | Deal lead opens: overall timeline status, process milestones, seller / banker dynamics |
| 0:10–0:40 | Workstream updates: each workstream lead provides status in 3–4 minutes. Format: status (on track / at risk / blocked), key findings to date, open items, next milestone |
| 0:40–0:50 | Emerging findings: any item that has changed the deal team's view of the deal or that needs IC visibility |
| 0:50–0:60 | Action items and decisions: owner, due date, escalation threshold |

**Meeting outputs:**
- DD status memo (2 pages max) distributed to Catherine Zhao and James Harrison after every meeting
- Action item tracker updated in real time
- Any finding that meets the red flag escalation criteria (Section 7) is flagged to IC the same day — it does not wait for the weekly memo

---

### 5.2 Escalation to IC

The IC interface during DD has two modes: **scheduled** and **immediate**.

**Scheduled IC touchpoints:**
- Day 30 synthesis meeting: Deal lead presents status to IC in person or by video. The IC decides whether to continue, restructure, or exit the deal.
- Day 55–60: Deal lead presents the completed DD summary section of the IC memo. IC provides final questions before the close vote.
- Close vote: Formal IC authorization to execute the purchase agreement.

**Immediate escalation (same-day):** Any red flag finding (Section 7) triggers a same-day call or meeting with Catherine Zhao (IC Chair) and the relevant Managing Partner. The deal lead does not allow a red flag finding to sit overnight.

**IC composition for DD decisions:** Catherine Zhao (IC Chair), James Harrison, Mark Sullivan, Priya Kapoor. Quorum for a go/no-go decision requires Catherine Zhao plus one other Managing Partner. All four are expected to be present for close authorization.

---

### 5.3 Data Room Management

**VDR platform:** Northwood uses a third-party virtual data room platform for all transactions. Access is provisioned by the deal lead and controlled throughout diligence.

**Access tiers:**

| Tier | Access Level | Members |
|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 — Full Access | All documents | Deal lead, CFO (Brian Taylor), external legal counsel, QofE advisor |
| Tier 2 — Workstream Access | Workstream-relevant documents only | IT advisor, environmental consultant, insurance advisor, HR advisor |
| Tier 3 — View Only / Named | Specific documents shared on request | Operating Partners (workstream-specific), other third-party advisors |

**Protocols:**
- The deal lead maintains the access log and reviews it weekly
- No documents are downloaded from the VDR without deal lead authorization
- Third-party advisors are granted access only under executed NDAs
- Seller's advisors do not have access to Northwood's internal workpapers or analysis
- Document production requests from management are tracked and aged — outstanding requests >5 business days after the initial DD request list go on the weekly DD status memo as open items

**DD request list:** The initial DD request list is issued to management within 48 hours of LOI execution. It is organized by workstream and prioritized (Priority 1: needed within Day 10; Priority 2: needed within Day 21; Priority 3: needed before close). The deal team does not wait for all documents to arrive before beginning analysis — workstreams run on available materials while continuing to track outstanding items.

## 6. Key Vendor / Advisor Relationships

Northwood maintains preferred advisor relationships across workstreams built through repeat transaction experience. Preferred advisors are not mandatory — deal-specific considerations (conflict checks, sector expertise, process timeline) may result in a different selection. But preferred advisors are the default for each category.

| Workstream | Preferred Advisor Category | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Quality of Earnings | Big 4 (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG) or Grant Thornton | Big 4 preferred for transactions above $150M EV. Grant Thornton engaged for transactions in the $75M–$150M EV range where Big 4 conflict checks have created availability issues. QofE advisor is never the same firm that audits the target company. |
| Legal (M&A) | Kirkland & Ellis, Latham & Watkins, or comparable M&A specialists | Mid-market M&A specialists preferred. Northwood does not use general practice firms for transaction legal work. |
| Tax | Integrated with QofE firm in most cases; separate tax counsel for complex structures (cross-border, multi-entity carve-outs, 338(h)(10) elections) | Brian Taylor manages tax advisor relationships directly. |
| IT / Cybersecurity | Specialized IT DD firms (e.g., Coalfire, Tevora, or comparable) | IT generalists are not adequate. Northwood requires advisors with PE-context DD experience and the ability to produce a clear investment-audience report, not a technical manual. |
| Environmental | Phase I / Phase II ESA specialists (e.g., AECOM, Terracon, ECS) with local market presence in the target geography | Environmental work requires on-site presence. National firms with local offices preferred. |
| Insurance | Insurance advisory firms with PE DD experience | Advisor is distinct from the R&W insurance carrier. They advise on coverage adequacy; they do not sell the policy. |
| Healthcare Reimbursement | Healthcare-specific advisory or Big 4 healthcare practice | Priya Kapoor maintains relationships with preferred advisors in this category. |
| Management Assessment | Leadership advisory firms with PE portfolio experience | Engaged selectively — primarily in founder-transition or management rebuild scenarios. |

**Engagement scope discipline:** Northwood scopes third-party advisors tightly. Advisors submit a draft scope of work before engagement letters are signed, and the deal lead reviews scope against the specific questions the workstream needs to answer. Open-ended time-and-materials engagements are not used — every engagement has a defined deliverable and a timeline. Cost overruns require deal lead approval.

## 7. Red Flag Escalation Protocol

Not every adverse finding requires immediate IC escalation. The escalation protocol distinguishes between findings that require deal team attention (normal course) and findings that require immediate IC involvement.

### 7.1 Normal Course Findings (deal team action, reported in weekly memo)

- QofE adjustments that reduce Adjusted EBITDA by less than 10% from management's representation
- Individual customer contracts with unfavorable terms that are not systemic
- IT infrastructure gaps that are addressable with defined capital investment
- HR issues that are manageable through employment law counsel
- Environmental findings that are minor and have defined remediation paths

### 7.2 Red Flag Findings (same-day IC escalation)

Any of the following require the deal lead to contact Catherine Zhao and the relevant Managing Partner the same day the finding is confirmed:

| Red Flag Category | Specific Triggers | Northwood Sector Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Revenue quality** | QofE finds >15% EBITDA reduction from management representation; revenue recognition practices that inflate LTM EBITDA; material undisclosed customer losses | Common in Business Services deals where channel revenue can be inflated through pull-forwards |
| **Litigation / regulatory** | Undisclosed material litigation; government investigation; regulatory enforcement action; license suspension or revocation | Healthcare deals: CMS or OIG audit findings, licensing deficiency; Industrial: EPA enforcement action |
| **Undisclosed liabilities** | Material tax liabilities, pension obligations, environmental remediation obligations, or warranty claims not disclosed in the VDR | Industrial Technology deals: undisclosed environmental contamination; all sectors: unpaid payroll taxes |
| **Management integrity** | Reference calls or background checks reveal misrepresentation in management's professional history; undisclosed conflicts of interest; prior fraud or regulatory sanction | Triggers immediate management assessment escalation; potential deal-breaker |
| **Customer concentration change** | Discovery that a top customer has terminated, issued notice of termination, or initiated a competitive re-bid that management did not disclose | Business Services and Healthcare Services most common exposure |
| **Reimbursement crisis (Healthcare)** | Payor audit demand; recoupment notice; CMS or state Medicaid finding that creates material liability | Project Horizon: payor compliance audit history requires same-day escalation if new findings emerge |
| **Cybersecurity / data breach** | Discovery of an undisclosed data breach or active cybersecurity incident | Any sector; severity elevated for Healthcare (HIPAA) and Business Services (client data custodian) |
| **Model-breaking finding** | Any finding that, if reflected in the model, reduces Base Case IRR below 18% gross | This is the return threshold below which the deal team does not have authorization to continue without IC direction |

### 7.3 Escalation Process

When a red flag finding is confirmed:

1. Deal lead contacts Catherine Zhao by phone — not by email
2. Deal lead prepares a one-page finding summary (what was found, source, estimated financial impact, deal implications) within 2 hours
3. Deal lead and Catherine Zhao determine whether to (a) continue with modified thesis, (b) re-price via renegotiation, (c) escalate to full IC for decision, or (d) exit the deal
4. If continuing: the red flag finding and resolution are documented in the DD summary memo and disclosed to IC at the Day 55–60 touchpoint regardless of resolution
5. If exiting: deal lead communicates the decision to the sell-side within 24 hours, with a professional rationale that does not disclose proprietary analysis

### 8.2 DD Summary Section (IC Memo Integration)

The DD Summary section of the IC memo is the deal lead's synthesis of all workstream findings for the Investment Committee. It is not a workstream-by-workstream enumeration — it is a structured argument about what diligence confirmed, what was refined, and what risks the IC is accepting by proceeding.

**Required structure:**

**DD Confirmed / Validated:** What was true in the underwrite at IC approval that remains true after full diligence? Name specific thesis elements with supporting evidence.

**DD Refined (Adjustments):** What changed as a result of diligence — EBITDA adjustments, deal structure changes, price adjustments, close conditions? Each item includes: (a) original assumption, (b) DD finding, (c) resolution.

**DD Identified Risks (Open at Close):** What risks were identified that are not fully resolved but are acceptable to carry through close? Each item includes: (a) risk description, (b) severity, (c) mitigation in place (contractual protection, indemnification, escrow, post-close monitoring plan).

**Residual Unknowns:** What was not fully diligenced — and why — and what is the plan to address it post-close?

### 8.3 Model Update Discipline

Every DD finding with a financial impact — positive or negative — is reflected in an updated model before the close vote. The model is not frozen at the IC approval stage. The model update memo (2–3 pages, authored by the deal lead) summarizes:

- EBITDA adjustments: starting EBITDA (management representation at IC approval) → DD-adjusted EBITDA, with a line-by-line bridge
- Revenue adjustments: if DD found customer losses, contract changes, or revenue quality issues that affect the revenue forecast
- Capex adjustments: deferred maintenance, IT capex, environmental remediation not previously reflected
- Return impact: Base case IRR and MOIC at DD-adjusted EBITDA vs. IC approval assumptions

The model update memo is circulated with the DD summary to IC at Day 55–60. The IC close vote is based on the DD-adjusted model, not the pre-DD model.
