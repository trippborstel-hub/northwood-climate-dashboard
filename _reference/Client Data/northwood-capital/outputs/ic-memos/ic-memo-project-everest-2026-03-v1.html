Section 2: Company Overview

2.1 Business History and Founding Narrative

Specialty Chemical Solutions was founded in 1985 by Richard Hoffman, then 22 years old, after three years as a technical sales engineer for a major chemical manufacturer. Hoffman identified that mid-market industrial customers were systematically underserved — national distributors prioritized their large accounts, and local distributors lacked technical expertise. He started with water treatment chemicals for industrial cooling systems, making deliveries himself from a rented 5,000 sq ft warehouse in Cincinnati. Revenue reached $8M by 1992 with 15 employees.

The Company has grown through four distinct phases: Foundation (1985–1992), Product Diversification (1993–2000), Regional Expansion (2001–2008), and Platform Maturity (2016–present). Key milestones include expansion into metalworking fluids, food-grade processing chemicals, and agricultural inputs; construction of a 25,000 sq ft custom blending facility; opening of four regional distribution warehouses; and the 2017 hire of Karen Walsh (VP Sales) to professionalize the commercial organization. SCS is now the largest independent specialty chemical distributor in the Ohio Valley region with $185.0M in LTM revenue.

Hoffman's decision to sell is straightforward: he is 63, has no children in the business, and has no identified successor. His wife's health is a secondary motivator for his stated 6–9 month exit timeline. This is a clean founder exit — no rollover, no part-time role. Hoffman has agreed to a 12–18 month post-close transition advisory arrangement covering customer relationship introductions and institutional knowledge transfer, compensated separately from transaction proceeds.

2.2 Business Model

SCS generates revenue through two mechanisms:

Distributed Products (80% of revenue): SCS acts as an authorized distributor for major chemical manufacturers (BASF, Dow, Ashland) — purchasing bulk chemicals and reselling in packaged formats to industrial customers. Gross margins on distributed products range 20–28% by category, with water treatment and food-grade at the high end and agricultural at the low end.

Custom and Private-Label Blends (20% of revenue): SCS formulates customer-specific chemical blends at its Cincinnati blending facility, using proprietary formulations developed in partnership with customers over years of technical engagement. Margins on custom blends are 400–600bps above distributed products. Critically, custom blends create formulation lock-in — switching requires customer re-qualification and testing, a process that takes 3–6 months and creates meaningful switching costs.

Revenue is predominantly repeat purchase (not contractually recurring). The Company operates on purchase order terms with annual master service agreements for its largest accounts. Renewal rates among the top 50 customers exceed 98%. Repeat purchase patterns are highly predictable — specialty chemicals are consumed in customers' daily manufacturing operations and represent essential process inputs.

Gross margin by product category:

  • Custom blends: 32–35%
  • Water treatment: 27–28%
  • Food & beverage: 25–27%
  • Metalworking: 24–26%
  • Institutional: 23–25%
  • Agricultural: 20–22%

Section 3: Market & Competitive Landscape

3.1 Market Size and Growth

The U.S. specialty chemical distribution market is estimated at $35–40B. Northwood underwrites the Ohio Valley and adjacent regional market as the Serviceable Addressable Market — approximately $3–5B, based on the 10–12% share of U.S. industrial production concentrated in the Ohio Valley corridor.

Market growth: 4–6% annually, driven by:

  • Industrial onshoring tailwind — U.S. manufacturing reshoring is increasing domestic chemical consumption, particularly in metalworking and process chemicals
  • Water treatment demand — aging infrastructure and EPA PFAS enforcement timeline are driving increased municipal and industrial water treatment spending
  • Regulatory complexity — increasing chemical management compliance requirements are pushing mid-market manufacturers to outsource to technical distributors rather than manage internally
  • Food safety regulation — FDA requirements for food-grade processing chemicals are driving demand growth in SCS's 18% food segment

Market growth has been 4–6% annually over the past decade [IBISWorld, Specialty Chemical Distribution in the US, Q4 2025]. SCS's 5-year revenue CAGR of ~5.4% is in line with market growth, indicating stable market share rather than gain or loss.

3.2 Market Structure

The specialty chemical distribution market is highly fragmented. The top 10 distributors collectively hold less than 25% market share. Hundreds of regional independent distributors operate in niche product categories and geographies. This fragmentation creates the consolidation opportunity that underpins the add-on thesis.

Key fragmentation dynamics:

  • Founder-owned regional independents ($10–100M revenue) represent the majority of the market
  • Geographic barriers: same-day/next-day delivery economics limit effective radius to 300–500 miles from distribution nodes
  • Product expertise barriers: specialty and technical categories require application knowledge that commodity-focused players lack
  • Consolidation has accelerated: PE-backed platforms are acquiring regional distributors at 5–7x EBITDA, creating meaningful arbitrage to platform multiples of 9–10x

Section 5: Investment Thesis

Thesis Pillar 1: Essential Products, Inelastic Demand, Recession-Resistant Revenue

What we believe: Specialty chemicals are consumed in customers' daily manufacturing and facility operations — they represent less than 3% of customer COGS but are critical process inputs without substitutes. Demand is structurally inelastic.

Evidence:

  • SCS revenue declined only 12% in the 2008–2009 recession vs. 20–30% for industrial distribution peers with greater capex exposure
  • 63% of revenue from water treatment, food processing, and institutional markets — essential services that do not discretionarily reduce chemical consumption
  • The cash conversion cycle (82 days) reflects consumption-based reorder patterns, not project-based demand
  • 40-year operating history through multiple economic cycles without a negative year above 12% revenue decline

Why we're right: The market often prices specialty chemical distributors at discounts to specialty services because "distribution" connotes commodity exposure. SCS's 40-year retention track record and recession performance demonstrate that the business behaves more like a specialty services company than a commodity distributor. We are buying this at 8.6x — consistent with chemical distribution — when the retention characteristics justify a services premium.

Value creation mechanism: Inelastic demand supports revenue visibility, which supports leverage at 3.4x with comfortable coverage. Debt paydown from FCF (projected $45M over 5 years) is high-confidence.

Key assumption to monitor: End market concentration in metalworking and agricultural (37% combined) creates moderate cyclicality exposure. Monitor monthly revenue by segment against prior-year trend.

Thesis Pillar 2: Founder Succession as Alpha — Professionalization Opportunity

What we believe: Hoffman's departure creates the primary risk and the primary value creation opportunity simultaneously. The business has under-invested in management infrastructure because Hoffman was the infrastructure. Installing a professional CEO, building institutional processes, and deploying Northwood's operational playbook will unlock value that founder-dependency has suppressed.

Evidence:

  • Pricing decisions, credit approvals above $50K, new product decisions, and supplier negotiations all funnel through Hoffman informally. There is no governance infrastructure around these decisions.
  • Walsh professionalized the sales organization from scratch after joining in 2017 — the gap between pre-Walsh and post-Walsh commercial operations demonstrates how much value professionalization creates at SCS.
  • EBITDA margins have expanded from 8.5% to 9.7% organically, primarily driven by Walsh's pricing discipline. Further expansion to 10–11% is achievable with systematic pricing management across all product categories.
  • Comparable distribution businesses with professional CEO and systematic commercial execution achieve 11–13% EBITDA margins vs. SCS at 9.7%. Closing this gap is worth $2–4M in EBITDA.

Why we're right: James Harrison and David Okafor are the right team for this transition. Harrison has executed 3 prior PE-backed CEO transitions in industrial businesses. Okafor's 25 years of industrial operations experience and prior CEO background provide credibility with the SCS operating team and customer base. Northwood's differentiated bid is not price — it's the specificity of our transition plan relative to sponsors without a defined operational playbook.

Value creation mechanism: Year 1 management action (CEO hire + Walsh expanded role) sets the foundation. EBITDA margin expansion from 9.7% to 10.8% by Year 5 driven by pricing discipline, procurement optimization, and SG&A leverage — worth approximately $4–5M in incremental EBITDA at exit.

Key assumption to monitor: CEO quality and cultural fit. The wrong CEO hire in a relationship-intensive distribution business can accelerate customer attrition rather than prevent it. The CEO search must be deeply informed by customer retention requirements, not just operational credentials.

Thesis Pillar 3: Add-On Platform with Identified Targets and Multiple Arbitrage

What we believe: The specialty chemical distribution market is highly fragmented ($35–40B, top 10 players at <25% market share), consolidation is active, and SCS's existing infrastructure can absorb geographic tuck-ins at 5–7x versus the 8.6x platform entry multiple.

Evidence:

  • 5 identified acquisition targets ($10–25M revenue, $0.8–2.5M EBITDA) in Cleveland, St. Louis, Detroit, Nashville, and Charlotte
  • Walsh has preliminary relationships with 2 of the 5 founders — reducing process friction and competitive bidding risk
  • PE-backed platforms are acquiring regional independents at 4–7x EBITDA currently (Baird comparable transaction data, Q4 2025)
  • SCS's distribution infrastructure (5 facilities, 42-truck fleet, blending capability) can absorb tuck-ins without proportional overhead additions — integration requires customer migration to SCS's ERP and sales management system, not new facility construction

Why we're right: The multiple arbitrage is real: buying at 5–7x and consolidating into an 8–10x platform creates $1–2M of value per acquisition at the portfolio company level before any operational synergy. With $25–35M in FCF over a 5-year hold and access to incremental term debt, 2 add-ons are financeable without additional equity contributions.

Value creation mechanism: Upside case adds $40M in combined revenue (2 acquisitions at ~$20M each) at blended 10–12% EBITDA margins. Combined with procurement synergies and sales force deployment, the add-ons are projected to contribute $4–5M in incremental EBITDA by Year 3 of the hold, supporting an exit EV of $342.0M at 9.5x vs. base case $229.5M at 9.0x. Upside returns of ~29% IRR / 3.6x MOIC exceed fund hurdles.

Key assumption to monitor: Target availability and pricing. Walsh has preliminary conversations but no signed LOIs. Independent validation of target founder motivation and timeline is required before the upside case is incorporated into the underwriting. The base case does not include add-ons.

Thesis Pillar 4: Custom Blending as Structural Moat

What we believe: SCS's custom blending business (20% of revenue, 400–600bps above average margins) creates formulation lock-in that fundamentally differentiates SCS from commodity distributors and supports long-term customer retention.

Evidence:

  • Switching from a SCS custom blend requires 3–6 months of re-qualification and testing — a significant operational disruption for customers
  • No customer using SCS custom blends has been lost to a competitor in 10+ years (management claim; to be validated in customer calls during DD)
  • The blending facility is operating at 70% capacity — meaning custom blend revenue can grow to 30%+ of total revenue without capital investment
  • Custom blend margins are 32–35% gross vs. 20–28% for distributed products — growing this mix is directly margin-accretive

Why we're right: Most chemical distributors at SCS's size do not invest in blending capability because it requires capital, technical expertise, and regulatory compliance infrastructure. This barrier — already built — represents 40 years of competitive investment that a new entrant cannot replicate quickly.

Value creation mechanism: Growing custom blends from 20% to 25–30% of revenue over the hold period adds 50–100bps to blended gross margins. At $185M revenue scaled to $237M by Year 5, this is worth $1.0–2.4M in incremental EBITDA.

Key assumption to monitor: Custom blend revenue growth requires sustained investment in technical sales capacity (application specialists). Headcount risk if Hoffman's departure disrupts the technical team.

Section 9: Key Risks & Mitigants

Risk 1: Founder/CEO Transition — Key Person Dependency

Critical Likely

Priority: Tier 1 — must be managed before and at close

Description: Richard Hoffman is the primary relationship holder for the top 10 accounts. Top 5 customers (32% of revenue) have direct, personal relationships with Hoffman spanning 15–22 years. He has no successor and is making a clean exit. The business has operated for 40 years with one leader whose departure creates simultaneous risks: customer relationship transfer failure, institutional knowledge loss, and cultural disruption.

Mitigants:

  • 12–18 month transition advisory agreement (Hoffman): Contractually obligates customer relationship introductions and knowledge transfer. Compensation structured separately from transaction proceeds to align incentives.
  • Joint customer visit plan: Hoffman + new CEO + Walsh will conduct structured joint visits with all top-20 accounts within the first 30 days post-close. The introduction of the new CEO under Hoffman's sponsorship is the highest-ROI activity in Year 1.
  • CEO profile aligned to relationship requirements: External CEO search will target distribution executives with existing relationships in the Ohio Valley industrial market — ideally someone known to one or more of SCS's top 10 customers.
  • Walsh as continuity bridge: Walsh has visited every top-50 customer at least quarterly for 9 years. She has the operating-level relationships; Hoffman has the C-suite relationships. Coordinated hand-off covers both levels.
  • David Okafor (Operating Partner) on-site during transition: Okafor's industrial background and CEO experience provides day-to-day operational credibility with the management team and workforce.

Residual risk: Even with these mitigants, some customer attrition is possible in Year 1. The model's downside case assumes loss of top 1–2 customers (9–17% of revenue), reducing EBITDA by $2.5–4.5M. This reduces returns to 1.2x / ~4% — capital preservation, not loss. Debt service remains covered in this scenario. [RC Assumption — customer loss estimate based on management call; Hoffman stated that top 5 customers "call me first." Walsh acknowledged the gap at C-suite level.]

Monitoring trigger: Customer churn rate among top 20 accounts; monthly revenue by customer in first 6 months post-close.

  • Director of Supply Chain hire (Month 1–3) will lead inventory optimization implementation

Residual risk: If no optimization is achieved, working capital remains at ~22% of revenue. Annual FCF is reduced by $1–2M relative to model. At base case EBITDA, this reduces deleveraging by ~10% over the hold, compressing exit equity by $2–3M. Returns impact: approximately (0.1x) MOIC. Not thesis-changing. [RC Assumption — sensitivity estimate based on model rework with and without optimization.]

Risk 3: Customer Concentration — Hidden or Overt

High Possible

Description: Top 5 customers represent 32% of revenue, with the top customer at 9%. The CIM presents customers as independent entities. The deal team has not yet validated corporate family trees for all top 20 accounts. A hidden concentration risk (related entities consolidated under common ownership) would materially change the concentration profile.

Mitigants:

  • Commercial DD workstream will include corporate family tree analysis (D&B/Capital IQ) for all top 20 accounts, requiring independent validation before final bid submission
  • All top 10 customers have been with SCS for 10+ years with 98%+ retention — churn risk is low even at the current concentration level
  • Customer tenures of 15–22 years and custom blend lock-in reduce the practical risk of sudden concentration-driven revenue loss

Residual risk: If hidden concentration is discovered (e.g., top 2 customers are subsidiaries of the same parent), effective top concentration increases meaningfully. This would trigger renegotiation of price or structure, not deal abandonment, given the custom blend lock-in and customer tenure. [RC Assessment — must resolve before final bid.]

Monitoring trigger: Commercial DD finding; any customer identified as a subsidiary of a top-10 account.

Risk 4: Environmental / Regulatory Exposure — Chemical Storage and Handling

High (potential) Remote to Possible

Description: SCS operates 5 facilities storing and distributing specialty chemicals, including hazardous, flammable, food-grade, and agricultural products. The Cincinnati HQ (150,000 sq ft, 40 years of operations) has the highest exposure risk. No pending regulatory actions or known liabilities, but a Phase I/II has not yet been conducted.

Mitigants:

  • Phase II assessments triggered if Phase I finds recognized environmental conditions at any site
  • SCS's current environmental compliance costs ($0.8M annually) reflect professional management — the business has invested in regulatory compliance, not deferred it
  • Angela Torres (Director of Quality & Compliance, 8 years) has managed EPA, DOT, and OSHA compliance without regulatory actions — institutional compliance quality is high

Residual risk: A material environmental finding at Cincinnati HQ (remediation cost $2–5M+) would require price renegotiation or escrow. The deal team has not modeled environmental remediation costs. R&W insurance with environmental coverage is strongly recommended. Any Phase II finding with remediation cost above $2.0M would trigger IC re-approval. [RC Assessment — must resolve before close.]

Monitoring trigger: Phase I/II environmental assessment findings; any EPA, state EPA, or municipal regulatory communication identified in legal DD.

Risk 5: Raw Material Cost Volatility

Medium Possible

Description: SCS passes through raw material cost increases with a 60–90 day lag. In a sustained inflationary cycle, gross margins compress temporarily by up to 100bps before pricing catches up. The 2022–2023 inflationary period is the most recent example.

Mitigants:

  • 60% of SCS's sourcing is from 3 major manufacturers (BASF, Dow, Ashland) with whom SCS has 20+ year relationships and authorized distributor status — providing priority allocation and reasonable pricing stability
  • Walsh has implemented systematic annual price increases in the sales organization, improving pass-through speed from informal to disciplined
  • Product mix diversification (water treatment, food, institutional, agricultural) reduces exposure to any single input commodity
  • Historical data confirms pricing catches up within 60–90 days — margin compression is temporary, not structural

Residual risk: A severe and sustained inflationary spike (similar to 2022) could compress EBITDA by $1.5–2.0M in Year 1. The model does not include a specific raw material inflation scenario but the base case Year 1 EBITDA of $19.5M (management projection) incorporates stable input costs. [RC Assumption — Year 1 downside sensitivity if inflation accelerates: -$1.5M EBITDA, which still yields positive FCF and interest coverage.]

Monitoring trigger: Monthly gross margin by product category; BASF/Dow/Ashland index pricing versus SCS list prices.

Thesis summary: SCS is a 40-year-old specialty chemical distributor with essential products, inelastic demand, 85%+ customer retention, custom blending lock-in, and a fragmented $35–40B market offering meaningful consolidation upside. The investment thesis is the convergence of two opportunities: (1) professionalization of a business that has outgrown its founder — capturing margin expansion and management infrastructure value that Hoffman's informal operating style has suppressed, and (2) geographic add-on platform build in a market with identified targets, proven multiple arbitrage (5–7x add-ons into an 8–10x platform), and Walsh already holding preliminary founder relationships.

On fund hurdles: The organic-only base case (~18% IRR / 2.3x MOIC) is below Northwood's 20%+ / 2.5x+ hurdle. The deal team recommends approval on the following basis: the base case is deliberately conservative (no credit for add-ons, no credit for multiple expansion beyond 0.4x), and the upside case (~29% / 3.6x) with 2 add-on acquisitions is well-supported by identified targets, proven market pricing, and Walsh's existing founder relationships. The deal team views the organic base case as a floor — a business of this quality, in a growing market, with a clear professionalization thesis and management equity alignment, should outperform flat multiple, organic-only assumptions.

Conditions on approval:

  1. QofE confirmation: Adjusted EBITDA must be confirmed within 10% of underwritten $18.0M by the QofE provider before final bid submission.
  2. Environmental clearance: No material Phase I findings triggering a Phase II with unacceptable remediation risk (threshold: $2.0M+ unbudgeted liability).
  3. Customer concentration validation: No hidden concentration in top 20 accounts that increases effective top-5 concentration above 40%.
  4. Walsh retention agreement: Executed prior to close as a closing condition.
  5. CEO search activation: CEO search engagement authorized immediately upon exclusivity grant; IC to receive search status update at April 18 IC meeting.
  6. Add-on commitment: Deal team commits to initiating formal add-on process (Targets A and B) within 18 months of close.

Primary concerns: Founder transition is the single highest-risk element of this investment. The team is confident in the mitigation plan (advisory agreement, joint visit protocol, Walsh as continuity bridge, Okafor as operational resource) but acknowledges that customer relationship transfer at this level of personal concentration is the most execution-intensive assumption in the thesis.
