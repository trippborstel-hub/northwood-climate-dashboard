Executive Summary

Recommendation: Advance with Conditions. Heartland Waste Solutions carries the highest ESG risk profile of any deal in Northwood's pipeline or portfolio history — a weighted composite of 3.7 with two dimensions scoring 5 (Environmental Liability, Labor Practices & Safety). Under the firm's ESG DD Assessment Protocol, this triggers a Presumptive Pass: the deal cannot advance unless the deal team demonstrates that (a) risk is fully quantifiable, (b) deal structure provides full protection, and (c) the value creation thesis justifies the risk premium.

The deal team rebuts the Presumptive Pass on all three grounds. Environmental liabilities are quantifiable within a $12.0M–$18.0M range supported by independent methodology. Deal structure protections — purchase price reduction, environmental escrow, seller indemnity, Pollution Legal Liability insurance, and close conditions for EHS and CFO hires — bound the downside. The buy-fix-sell thesis generates a base case 4.2x MOIC / ~33% IRR at an ESG-adjusted entry of 6.7x, with the bear case (remediation at high end, Belvidere expansion denied) still producing a 2.7x / ~22% return that meets Northwood's floor.

This memo presents the full ESG assessment, details the six conditions required for IC advancement, and identifies four open items that must be resolved during post-LOI diligence.

Detailed Findings by Dimension

E3 — Environmental Liability (Score: 5 — High)

Environmental liability is the defining risk of this transaction. It is not a single issue — it is a pattern of deferred environmental management accumulated over 23 years of founder-operator ownership, now crystallized in regulatory enforcement actions across multiple jurisdictions.

Belvidere Landfill — IL EPA Consent Order

The Illinois EPA issued a consent order in October 2024 against the Belvidere landfill for groundwater contamination. PFAS was detected at 28 ppt — 7x the EPA maximum contaminant level of 4 ppt. Approximately 20 groundwater monitoring wells surround the site [observed during Feb 12 facility visit], several with recent IL EPA sampling tags.

The contamination predates HWS ownership. Kowalski acquired the Belvidere site in 2005 from a prior operator that had a chemical storage operation [Mgmt Meeting, Feb 12]. However, the liability follows the property. HWS is the responsible party under the consent order regardless of the contamination's origin.

The consent order requires a remediation plan by July 2026 — 4 months from today. Management has retained Midwest Environmental Associates as the environmental consultant but has not yet defined the PFAS remediation scope [Mgmt Meeting, Feb 12]. Kowalski's verbal remediation estimate of $4.0M–$6.0M [Mgmt Meeting, Feb 12] is anchored on costs experienced to date (monitoring, consulting, soil work) rather than on the full remediation scope that PFAS contamination at 7x the regulatory limit requires. The CIM's stated range of $8.0M–$12.0M [CIM, Harris Williams, Jan 2026] is more realistic but likely still understated.

Janesville Landfill — WI DNR Warning

The Wisconsin DNR issued a warning letter in March 2025 requiring a leachate collection system upgrade at the Janesville landfill. Management estimates $2.0M–$3.0M for the upgrade [Mgmt Meeting, Feb 12] and has been deferring the work. This is a known, bounded obligation with a defined technical scope — lower risk than Belvidere but indicative of the pattern.

Closed Sites

Three closed disposal sites carry 30-year post-closure monitoring obligations at ~$450K/year [CIM, Jan 2026]. One site has unresolved soil contamination — Kowalski did not identify which site or specify the nature or extent [Mgmt Meeting, Feb 12]. This is an open item requiring Phase I/II investigation.

Remediation Reserve

There is no remediation reserve on the balance sheet. Janet Polanski (Controller) confirmed that environmental costs are expensed as incurred from operating cash flow with no accrual for future obligations [Mgmt Meeting, Feb 12]. When presented with a $12.0M–$15.0M potential liability, her response was: "That... that would be a lot. We have about $4 million in cash at any time." Cash basis accounting makes the entire environmental liability invisible in the company's financial records.

S3 — Community & Reputation (Score: 4 — Elevated)

Community opposition to the Belvidere landfill expansion is organized and growing. Of 87 public comments received on the expansion application, 72 were opposed (83%) [Initial Screen, Jan 28, 2026]. Residential development sits approximately 0.5 miles from the landfill boundary. At least 4 hand-painted "NO EXPANSION" signs were visible from the landfill access road during the February 12 site visit. Landfill odor was noticeable from approximately 0.5 miles [Site Visit Observations, Feb 12].

The public hearing is scheduled for April 2026. If the expansion is denied, Belvidere has 4.2 years of remaining airspace [CIM, Jan 2026]. Denial would transform the Belvidere landfill from a 15+ year permitted asset into a depleting resource, materially changing the business plan, exit narrative, and valuation.

The 2024 fatality received local press coverage, adding to the community's negative perception of HWS operations. The combination of environmental contamination, odor complaints, a workplace fatality, and public regulatory enforcement creates a reputational profile that a buyer inherits at close.

Recommendation and Rebuttal

Recommendation: Advance with Conditions

The ESG composite score of 3.7 with two dimensions at 5 triggers a Presumptive Pass under the ESG DD Assessment Protocol (Section 5, Decision Thresholds). The presumptive pass is rebuttable if the deal team demonstrates that (a) risk is fully quantifiable, (b) deal structure provides full protection, and (c) the value creation thesis justifies the risk premium.

Rebuttal — Three Conditions Met

(a) Risk is quantifiable. Environmental liabilities are bounded within a $12.0M–$18.0M range. The range is driven by PFAS remediation at Belvidere (uncertain but bracketed by comparable site data), Janesville leachate upgrade ($2.0M–$3.0M with defined technical scope), and closed site soil remediation ($1.0M–$2.0M). Safety and governance costs are independently estimable from market compensation data and program build costs. The sensitivity analysis (see ESG Valuation Impact Analysis) shows that even at $22.0M remediation (25% above high-end estimate) and an 8.5x exit, the fund achieves a 2.1x MOIC.

(b) Deal structure provides protection. The ESG DD Assessment Protocol (Section 6) defines the required protections. For Greenfield, these are:

  • Purchase price reduction: Entry at 6.5x–7.0x on QofE-adjusted EBITDA (vs. 8.5x clean comp) — a $22.5M–$27.5M discount from clean valuation that funds the remediation.
  • Environmental escrow: $18.0M (independent estimate, high case). Released upon remediation completion or regulatory closure.
  • Seller environmental indemnity: Pre-close contamination. Survival: longer of 5 years or regulatory closure + 12 months.
  • Pollution Legal Liability (PLL) insurance: Sized to regulatory worst-case. 10-year term minimum.
  • R&W carve-out: Known environmental conditions excluded from R&W coverage; addressed by PLL.
  • Close conditions: EHS Director identified (offer extended), CFO identified (offer extended).

(c) Value creation thesis justifies the risk premium. This is a buy-fix-sell thesis, not a platform build. The investment return is directly produced by ESG remediation — the ESG discount at entry and the ESG fix at exit are the investment. In the base case, $40.0M of fund equity generates a 4.2x MOIC / ~33% IRR. 88% of the return comes from EBITDA growth and multiple expansion, both directly driven by the ESG remediation. The bear case (2.7x / ~22%) meets Northwood's 2.5x / 20% return floor. A traditional risk-adjusted buyer will not underwrite this deal — Northwood's ability to execute the ESG fix is the competitive advantage.
